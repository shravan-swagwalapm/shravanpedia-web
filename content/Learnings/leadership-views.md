> "Beyond products, building leaders."

## Context

Across 789 LinkedIn posts on leadership and culture, Shravan's views are shaped by three overlapping roles: 9 years as a product leader in companies, founder of [[rethink-systems]], and mentor to 400+ operators worldwide. His views are not management-theory abstractions -- they come from running cohorts, managing mentors, and watching students transform. The through-line is that leadership is a human game first and a professional one second.

## Details

### The AI-Age Leader

Shravan's most urgent and repeated message from 2024-2026 is that the leadership landscape has fundamentally shifted. "You're no longer competing with just people. You're competing with PMs and engineers who wield AI like a cheat code and can ship in half the time at a fraction of the cost." His response is not panic but clarity: stop scrolling reels and start sharpening your AI skills. He curates extensive lists of AI PM resources -- 19 videos, meticulously selected from leaders at Google, Uber, Microsoft, Meta, and Duolingo.

But the key insight is not "learn AI tools." It is that AI is already rewriting the present, and leaders who ignore this are not behind -- they are already irrelevant. His framing for AI and leadership within Rethink Systems: "We didn't add AI as a topic. We wove it into every PM decision." The leader's job is not to use AI but to think clearly with AI as a co-builder.

### Building Leaders, Not Training PMs

The Rethink Systems NPS numbers -- 92+ overall, 94-100 instructor NPS across all cohorts -- tell a story, but Shravan cares more about a different metric. When his students say "you duped us into learning human values instead of product management," that is the signal he is looking for.

He explicitly frames the program as a leadership incubator: "In just 8 intense weeks, 60 extraordinary individuals didn't just upskill as PMs -- they transformed into sharper thinkers, empathetic leaders, and better human beings." The selection is intentionally hard, with less than 10% acceptance rate, because density of quality matters more than volume. The result is a community that stays with you long after the program ends -- alumni who mentor, hire, and open doors for each other.

### Credit Generosity as a Leadership Principle

From his PM anti-patterns writing, but fundamentally a leadership principle: "Be generous with credit -- your team did more than you think, and you did less than you realize." Shravan consistently names every mentor, every guest speaker, every student contributor in his posts. The tagging is not performative networking -- it is a deliberate practice of making invisible work visible, grounded in the belief that leadership is measured by how much credit you give away, not how much you accumulate.

### Suggestions Over Instructions

A key distinction Shravan draws repeatedly: great leaders give suggestions, not instructions. The difference between "How did you pick X over say, Y or Z for this?" and "Y is better than X, let's change it" is the difference between a leader who builds judgment in others and one who creates dependency. "An inflated level of competence leads to regularly stepping into the shoes of a dev or designer. It may seem a good thing first but becomes a liability soon. To scale up, the PM has to learn to give suggestions instead of instructions."

### Influence Without Authority

He recommends *Team of Rivals* as "the best book written on how to influence without authority," and the framing is not accidental. Shravan sees leadership as fundamentally about influence, not position. The PM role is his proof case: you are not the CEO, but you must think like a founder. You have no authority over engineers or designers, but you must create alignment. The ability to influence without authority is not a nice-to-have for PMs -- it is the entire job description, and it is what separates the PMs people want to work with from the ones they resent.

### The Community as Leadership Model

[[rethink-systems]] has become Shravan's living experiment in what leadership culture looks like at scale. The community is global by default, spanning 15+ countries with 20% international students. Selection is meritocratic with less than 10% acceptance rate, ensuring quality density. The mentor-as-alumni pipeline turns students into mentors who become community leaders, creating a self-sustaining cycle. Multi-day Bangalore retreats achieve NPS of 80+, judged by industry leaders. Guest masters include SVPs and CPOs from Zee, Adobe, Amazon, LaunchDarkly, and Cashfree.

"This community at Rethink Systems is a testament to kindness, generosity, and grit coming together."

### The Indian Creator-Leader

His posts about Razorpay reveal a dimension often missing from leadership discourse: the reality of building as an Indian creator with a global audience. Forex markups, platform cuts, GST compliance, payment delays. "Hum course sikha rahe hain, par lagta hai hum hi fees de rahe hain." He advocates loudly: "Indian creators deserve clarity. Indian freelancers deserve speed. Indian businesses deserve respect." For Shravan, leadership includes fighting systemic friction, not just inspiring teams. A leader who ignores the infrastructure problems around their people is not really leading.

### Layoffs as a Leadership Failure

His repeated framing of mass layoffs -- 200K+ across Amazon, Google, Microsoft, IBM, and Oracle in 2024-2026 -- is pointed: "Not because people failed but because they assumed AI was the future, when in reality, it's already rewriting our present." The implication is clear -- institutional leadership failed these people by not adapting fast enough. Shravan's response is to equip individuals with the skills to be layoff-proof, because he does not trust institutional leadership to protect careers. The bet is on the individual, armed with AI skills and first-principles thinking, rather than on the corporation.

## Impact

Shravan's leadership philosophy is inseparable from his teaching practice. He does not theorize about leadership -- he runs a 400+ person community that embodies his principles daily. The evidence is in the NPS scores, the career transitions, and the fact that graduates stay connected as family rather than alumni. His distinctive contribution to the leadership conversation: leadership is not a corporate skill but a human one, rooted in empathy, generosity with credit, and the courage to do boring things consistently. The AI age has not changed that -- it has only made it more urgent.
