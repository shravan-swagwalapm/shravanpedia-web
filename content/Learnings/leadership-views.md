> "Beyond products, building leaders."

## Context

Across 789 LinkedIn posts on leadership and culture, Shravan's views are shaped by three overlapping roles: 9 years as a product leader in companies (Flipkart, Edureka, BlackBuck, BYJU'S, Bhanzu), founder of [[rethink-systems]], and mentor to 400+ operators worldwide. His views are not management-theory abstractions lifted from business school case studies. They come from the daily reality of running cohorts, managing mentors, watching students transform, navigating cross-cultural teams, and building a company where the product is human development. The through-line is that leadership is a human game first and a professional one second — and the leaders who forget this are the ones whose teams quietly start updating their resumes.

## Details

### The AI-Age Leader

Shravan's most urgent and repeated message from 2024-2026 is that the leadership landscape has fundamentally shifted, and the shift is not coming — it has already happened. "You're no longer competing with just people. You're competing with PMs and engineers who wield AI like a cheat code and can ship in half the time at a fraction of the cost." His response is not panic but clarity: stop scrolling reels and start sharpening your AI skills. He curates extensive lists of AI PM resources — 19 videos, meticulously selected from leaders at Google, Uber, Microsoft, Meta, and Duolingo — not as a reading list but as an action plan.

But the key insight is deeper than "learn AI tools." It is that AI is already rewriting the present, and leaders who ignore this are not behind — they are already irrelevant. The window for catching up is narrowing. His framing for AI and leadership within Rethink Systems: "We didn't add AI as a topic. We wove it into every PM decision." Batch 7 of the MPM cohort was rebuilt as "Product x AI edition," not because AI was trendy but because building in 2026 without AI fluency is like building in 2006 without internet fluency — technically possible but professionally untenable.

The leader's job in this landscape is not to use AI but to think clearly with AI as a co-builder. The distinction matters. Using AI is a skill that can be learned in a weekend. Thinking clearly with AI — knowing when to trust the output, when to override it, when to ask a different question, when to build versus when to buy — is judgment that takes years to develop. This judgment is what Shravan teaches, and it is what separates his approach from the flood of "AI for PMs" content that focuses on prompting techniques rather than strategic thinking.

### Building Leaders, Not Training PMs

The Rethink Systems NPS numbers — 92+ overall, 94-100 instructor NPS across all cohorts — tell a story of satisfied students, but Shravan cares more about a different metric. When his students say "you duped us into learning human values instead of product management," that is the signal he is looking for. It means the deeper curriculum worked — the one that is not on the syllabus.

He explicitly frames the program as a leadership incubator: "In just 8 intense weeks, 60 extraordinary individuals didn't just upskill as PMs — they transformed into sharper thinkers, empathetic leaders, and better human beings." The selection is intentionally hard, with less than 10% acceptance rate, because density of quality matters more than volume. When every person in a cohort of 25 is a serious operator — a VP at Barclays, a Product Lead at Google, a Director at Mastercard — the peer learning creates a gravitational pull that no curriculum alone can produce. The result is a community that stays with you long after the program ends — alumni who mentor, hire, and open doors for each other across companies spanning three continents.

### Credit Generosity as a Leadership Principle

From his PM anti-patterns writing, but fundamentally a leadership principle: "Be generous with credit — your team did more than you think, and you did less than you realize." Shravan consistently names every mentor, every guest speaker, every student contributor in his posts. He tags collaborators. He highlights individual contributions. He goes out of his way to make invisible work visible.

This is not performative networking or LinkedIn engagement optimization. It is a deliberate practice grounded in the belief that leadership is measured by how much credit you give away, not how much you accumulate. The practice creates a specific cultural effect: when the leader models credit generosity, the team models it too. The Rethink community's culture of mutual support — alumni helping alumni, mentors spending midnight hours with students in different time zones — traces directly to this principle being modeled at the top.

### Suggestions Over Instructions

A key distinction Shravan draws repeatedly: great leaders give suggestions, not instructions. The difference between "How did you pick X over say, Y or Z for this?" and "Y is better than X, let's change it" is the difference between a leader who builds judgment in others and one who creates dependency. The first question invites the team member to explain their reasoning, which either reveals sound thinking (in which case the leader learns something) or exposes a gap (in which case the teaching moment is organic rather than imposed).

"An inflated level of competence leads to regularly stepping into the shoes of a dev or designer. It may seem a good thing first but becomes a liability soon. To scale up, the PM has to learn to give suggestions instead of instructions." The warning is pointed: the PM who can do everyone's job often ends up doing everyone's job, and that is not leadership — it is a bottleneck with a title. True leadership is multiplying the team's capability, not demonstrating your own.

### Influence Without Authority

He recommends *Team of Rivals* as "the best book written on how to influence without authority," and the framing is not accidental. Shravan sees leadership as fundamentally about influence, not position. The PM role is his proof case: you are not the CEO, but you must think like a founder. You have no authority over engineers or designers, but you must create alignment across teams that have their own priorities and their own incentives.

The ability to influence without authority is not a nice-to-have for PMs — it is the entire job description. It requires understanding what motivates each stakeholder, finding the shared objective that aligns different interests, and communicating in a way that makes collaboration feel like everyone's idea rather than one person's directive. Lincoln did this with a cabinet full of rivals who each believed they should be president instead of him. The best PMs do it every day with engineering managers who have their own roadmaps and designers who have their own vision.

### The Community as Leadership Model

[[rethink-systems]] has become Shravan's living experiment in what leadership culture looks like at scale — a 400+ person community that embodies the principles he teaches. The community is global by default, spanning 15+ countries with 20% international students. Selection is meritocratic with less than 10% acceptance rate, ensuring quality density. The mentor-as-alumni pipeline turns students into mentors who become community leaders, creating a self-sustaining cycle.

Multi-day Bangalore retreats achieve NPS of 80+, judged by industry leaders. Guest masters include SVPs and CPOs from Zee, Adobe, Amazon, LaunchDarkly, and Cashfree — people who come not because they are paid but because the community's energy is worth their time. "This community at Rethink Systems is a testament to kindness, generosity, and grit coming together."

The community functions as both proof and laboratory. It proves that the leadership principles work because 400+ operators live by them daily. And it serves as a laboratory because each cohort, each retreat, each mentor interaction generates data about what makes communities thrive and what causes them to decay. The data feeds back into the teaching, which feeds back into the community, which generates more data.

### The Indian Creator-Leader

His posts about Razorpay reveal a dimension often missing from leadership discourse: the reality of building as an Indian creator with a global audience. Forex markups. Platform cuts. GST compliance. Payment delays. "Hum course sikha rahe hain, par lagta hai hum hi fees de rahe hain" — "We're teaching courses, but it feels like we're paying the fees ourselves." The frustration is real: Indian creators face a structural disadvantage in the global creator economy, paying more in transaction costs, waiting longer for payments, and navigating a regulatory environment designed for large corporations rather than solo operators.

He advocates loudly: "Indian creators deserve clarity. Indian freelancers deserve speed. Indian businesses deserve respect." For Shravan, leadership includes fighting systemic friction, not just inspiring teams. A leader who ignores the infrastructure problems around their people is not really leading — they are giving speeches while the plumbing leaks. His willingness to name these problems publicly, to call out specific platforms and specific policies, is itself a leadership act: using his platform (194K+ LinkedIn followers) to advocate for the community he serves.

### Layoffs as a Leadership Signal

His repeated framing of mass layoffs — 200K+ across Amazon, Google, Microsoft, IBM, and Oracle in 2024-2026 — is pointed: "Not because people failed but because they assumed AI was the future, when in reality, it's already rewriting our present." The implication is clear — institutional leadership was too slow to adapt, and the people who paid the price were the ones with the least power to change direction.

Shravan's response is to equip individuals with the skills to be resilient regardless of institutional decisions, because he does not trust institutional leadership to protect careers. The bet is on the individual — armed with AI skills, first-principles thinking, and the ability to build — rather than on the corporation. The [[predict-my-layoff]] project is the product expression of this philosophy: give individuals the data they need to assess their own risk, rather than waiting for their employer to be honest with them.

### Empathy as Operational Leadership

Running the MPM cohort has given Shravan firsthand experience with a dimension of leadership that corporate roles rarely expose: the moment when a student breaks down during a session, when a mentee's family situation disrupts their progress, when the "professional" and "personal" become impossible to separate. His response in these moments reveals the Gita's influence — treating each person as a complete human, not a resource, and responding to their situation with the same earnestness he would want for himself.

The Rethink community's culture of care — the midnight mentor calls, the alumni who fly to Bangalore for retreats, the spontaneous congratulations for incoming students — is not an accident of personality. It is the natural output of a leadership model that treats empathy as an operational skill rather than a soft skill. Empathy in this context is not "I understand how you feel." It is "I understand your situation well enough to help you navigate it." The difference is the gap between sympathy and action, and Shravan's leadership consistently lands on the action side.

## Impact

Shravan's leadership philosophy is inseparable from his teaching practice. He does not theorize about leadership — he runs a 400+ person community that embodies his principles daily. The evidence is in the NPS scores, the career transitions, the alumni who stay connected as family, and the culture that sustains itself without constant intervention from the founder. His distinctive contribution to the leadership conversation: leadership is not a corporate skill but a human one, rooted in empathy, generosity with credit, the courage to do boring things consistently, and the spiritual conviction that serving others is not a strategy but a dharma. The AI age has not changed that — it has only made it more urgent.
