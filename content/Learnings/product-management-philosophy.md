> "You don't need a lot of frameworks to become a better PM, you just have to be a better human to become a better PM."

## Context

Product management is Shravan's core professional identity — 9+ years as a product leader across Flipkart, Edureka, BlackBuck, BYJU'S, and Bhanzu; 1,983 LinkedIn posts on the topic (the most-posted-about subject by a wide margin); a YouTube channel ([[the-swagwala-pm]]); and a full education company ([[rethink-systems]]) built around teaching it. His PM philosophy is not academic. It was forged through real hiring conversations where the polished candidate lost to the passionate one, through real product launches where the technically perfect feature was ignored because it solved the wrong problem, and through watching hundreds of students transform from framework-dependent aspirants into first-principles operators who can navigate any product situation because they understand why the frameworks exist, not just how to use them.

## Details

### The Founder Mindset

The single strongest conviction across all of Shravan's PM posts is that **a PM should think and act like a founder** — taking ownership as if the product were their own, not managing it from a safe distance.

He tells a defining story repeatedly — posted five or more times between 2022 and 2026 — because it encodes his deepest belief about what makes a PM exceptional. After a difficult product sense interview, Shravan did not accept the rejection as final. Instead, he spent 15 hours doing deep research on the company's product, wrote a comprehensive PRD that nobody asked for, and sent it to the founder. The founder called him back.

"Lakde, maine zindagi main ek cheez seekhi hai — jo log apne passion ke liye sab kuch peeche chod dete hai, woh log zindagi main zaroor kuch acha karte hai." (Kid, I've learned one thing in life — people who give up everything for their passion always do something good.)

The lesson he extracts every time he tells it: "I didn't hire you because of your PRD. I looked at your attempt to genuinely solve a problem comprehensively in a really pressured scenario." The founder did not evaluate the document. He evaluated the behavior. The PRD was evidence of a mindset — the willingness to go unreasonably deep, to do work nobody requested, to treat someone else's product as if it were your own. This is founder mentality applied to a job interview, and it is the behavioral pattern Shravan looks for in every student, every mentee, and every founder he invests in.

PMs must take initiative as if the product is theirs. Passion and persistence beat polish. This story functions as his origin myth because it captures the essence of his entire philosophy in a single narrative: the PM who cares enough to do the unreasonable thing is the PM who changes the game.

### PM Anti-Patterns

Shravan wrote a detailed taxonomy of why colleagues end up hating PMs, and it reads like a mirror held up to the profession — uncomfortable, precise, and impossible to dismiss.

**The Know-it-all** operates with an inflated level of competence, giving instructions instead of suggestions. The correction is subtle but transformative: "How did you pick X over Y?" is a suggestion that builds the team's judgment. "Y is better, let's change it" is an instruction that creates dependency. The first question assumes the team member had reasons. The second assumes they did not.

**The 30K Feet PM** takes vague requests from leadership and throws them across the fence to engineering without adding clarity. The PM's job is not to relay — it is to translate. A vague request from a VP must become a clear problem statement, a defined scope, and a set of success criteria before engineering ever sees it. The PM who fails to do this translation is not delegating — they are abdicating.

**The Indecisive PM** cannot stop adding scope — "Let's add one more feature!" — and puts the creep in scope creep. Every feature added delays the ship date, introduces complexity, and dilutes the core value proposition. The indecisive PM thinks they are being thorough. They are actually being afraid — afraid to ship, afraid to be judged, afraid of the version of the product that is only 80% of their vision.

**The Visionary PM** hoards credit and deflects blame, embodying the principle that "vision without execution is mere hallucination." The vision is important, but the PM who claims the vision as a personal accomplishment while blaming the team for execution shortfalls has fundamentally misunderstood their role.

**The CEO/Deadline Tyrant** does not code or design but is supremely confident about how long things take, ignoring that estimates are hard and the devil lives in the implementation details that are invisible from a product brief.

His antidote cuts across all five: "Don't act like the CEO. Make your invisible work visible. Share your level of conviction. Be generous with credit — your team did more than you think, and you did less than you realize."

### Compassion as Product Thinking

One of Shravan's most distinctive contributions to PM philosophy is the claim that product sense comes from **compassion, not frameworks** — and that this is not a soft sentiment but a practical, observable truth.

He tells a story about rescuing an injured stray dog that led him to build a curated pet adoption marketplace with the animal husbandry department. The progression was organic: compassion for the animal led to curiosity about the problem (how many strays exist, what happens to them, who is responsible), which led to passion for testing solutions at scale, which led to critical thinking about whether the core hypothesis was correct. He named this the **Compassion Funnel**: compassion → curiosity → passion → critical thinking.

"It's the art of solving problems at scale but also ensuring it builds a happiness layer for the user." This framing is not sentimental decoration layered onto a conventional PM curriculum. It is a genuine alternative to the frameworks-first approach that dominates PM education. Most PM programs start with analytics, user stories, and roadmapping. Shravan starts with the claim that if you do not feel something about the problem you are solving — if you cannot trace your interest back to compassion for the people affected — then the product will be technically competent and emotionally empty, and emotionally empty products do not earn loyalty. ### The PM Specialization Taxonomy

The MPM session notes from Week 2 reveal a detailed taxonomy of PM types that Shravan teaches as a systems-thinking exercise. The framing is revelatory: each type of PM is fundamentally a systems builder — the prefix simply defines which system they optimize.

The **Technical Product Manager** builds the tech system — architecture, APIs, infrastructure — translating business problems into technical requirements. Shravan explicitly distinguishes TPMs from Product Owners: TPMs are strategic and involved in discovery, while POs focus on execution without discovery or strategy. The distinction matters because many organizations use the titles interchangeably, and the confusion produces role ambiguity that makes everyone's job harder.

The **Data Product Manager** builds the data system — pipelines, warehousing, dashboards — following a five-step workflow: identify what data to capture based on the user journey, define data nomenclature, perform ETL, normalize inconsistencies, and build the business intelligence layer. This is not a glamorous description, but it is an honest one, and honesty about what each PM type actually does is more useful than aspirational job descriptions.

The **Growth Product Manager** builds the growth system — loops, referrals, virality — the system that makes the product distribute itself. See [[growth-loops-and-defensible-growth]] for the full treatment.

Shravan's meta-insight ties them together: "The prefix just defines which system you're optimizing." Every PM is a systems builder. This framing connects PM career identity directly to [[systems-thinking]] rather than treating specializations as siloed domains that require different skill sets. A TPM and a Growth PM share far more than they differ — both are building systems, both need first-principles thinking, both need the ability to navigate ambiguity.

The notes also codify a critical distinction between **functional skills** and **principle skills**. Functional skills are domain knowledge — payments, edtech, healthcare — and they get you hired. Principle skills are first-principles thinking, discovery, insight-generation, and communication — and they help you thrive and grow. Most PM courses teach only functional skills because they are easier to package. The MPM cohort deliberately weights principle skills because they transfer across domains and compound over time. A PM who masters payment rails can work in fintech. A PM who masters first-principles thinking can work anywhere.

### AI as a Second Brain, Not a Replacement

His 2025-2026 evolution reframes AI's role for product managers in a way that cuts through the noise of "AI for PMs" content flooding LinkedIn. "Building in 2026 is not about who knows prompting. It's about who can think clearly with AI as a co-builder." He positions AI as a "side brain" — a powerful tool that amplifies human judgment but cannot replace it.

His program, MPM 2.0, weaves AI into every PM decision: discovery, execution, analytics, storytelling. The overarching conviction: "Great PMs don't just ship features, they ship trust." AI can help you ship faster, but it cannot help you ship the right thing — that requires the product judgment that only comes from compassion, curiosity, and deep understanding of the user.

The Week 4 session notes elaborate this into a nuanced AI fluency framework. Shravan distinguishes between the **service layer** of AI (using tools like ChatGPT and Perplexity to accomplish tasks) and the **product layer** (understanding how AI works under the hood — why models hallucinate, what influences outputs, how to design user flows involving probabilistic outcomes). The service layer is table stakes. The product layer is the differentiator.

He teaches that AI systems are fundamentally probabilistic, not deterministic — every output is a likely guess, not a guaranteed fact. He introduces the concept of **regret ratio** as a model's level of incorrectness over time. The practical implication for PMs is stark: "Most PMs focus on the value of AI. But few design for the limitations of AI." The PM who understands hallucination, drift, and confidence calibration builds better AI products than the PM who treats the model as a black box.

He draws a clear boundary: AI is useful for speed, not for first-principle reasoning. "If you're letting AI do the thinking, then what are you doing?" Prompt engineering is taught as a genuine engineering discipline — comparable to trial-and-error iteration in hardware — requiring structured instructions with output clarity, constraints, context, and return format. ### Teaching as Mission

He started [[the-swagwala-pm]] YouTube channel in 2021, and the metric that matters to him is not subscriber count but impact: 200+ PMs transitioned into roles free of cost, 500+ through Rethink Systems, 10,000+ mentored overall. He curates structured 5-week playlists and recommends books — always including the Bhagavad Gita alongside *Inspired* by Marty Cagan, never as a separate category.

His sign-off is always "Yours, Shravan Bhaiya," and the positioning is deliberate. Not professor, not guru, not sir. **Bhaiya** — older brother. Accessible, personal, relational. The way an older brother teaches is different from how a professor lectures: it is patient, it is honest, it assumes you will eventually get it, and it never makes you feel stupid for not getting it yet. The sign-off is not a branding choice. It is a relationship declaration.

### The Rethink Systems Model

[[rethink-systems]] was built after 9 years in product leadership, and it embodies every principle in this article. Bootstrapped to $1M+ in revenue. No VC, no board meetings, no growth targets imposed from outside. 400+ operators across 15+ countries. NPS of 92+ across every cohort, with Instructor NPS reaching 100. His framing: "This is not a program but the path to the best versions of yourself."

His students from Batch 6 said something that clearly moved him deeply: "Shravan, you have duped us into learning human values instead of product management." He took it as the highest possible compliment, and his response captures the entire philosophy: "This community was never only about learning PM — it was about unlearning mistakes from the past, becoming a better human every day, forgiving yourself, and building a brighter future."

The statement reveals the true curriculum. The PM frameworks — discovery, roadmapping, analytics, design thinking — are the visible layer. The invisible layer — the one that produces behavioral transformation — is the [[three-superpowers]] (gratitude, unlearning, compassion), the [[commanders-intent]] framework, and the spiritual foundation drawn from [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]]. The visible layer teaches students to be better PMs. The invisible layer teaches them to be better humans. And Shravan's deepest conviction is that you cannot achieve the first without the second.

## Impact

PM is not just what Shravan does — it is the lens through which he sees every problem, from angel investing decisions to spiritual practice to health transformations. His philosophy is distinctly humanistic in a field that trends toward frameworks and metrics. He teaches product sense through compassion, positions the Gita alongside Marty Cagan, and measures success in humans helped rather than revenue generated.

The founder mindset story is his origin myth — told and retold because it encodes his deepest belief: passion and genuine problem-solving outweigh credentials every time. The Compassion Funnel is his most original contribution to PM thinking — the claim that product sense starts not with data but with caring. The PM anti-patterns are his most honest teaching — the uncomfortable truth that many PMs are disliked because they deserve to be. And the Rethink Systems model is his proof: a $1M+ education company, bootstrapped, where the students leave saying they learned to be better humans, and where that is the point.
