> A referral program driven by external rewards is not a loop. Loops are about gaining users organically, by design.

## Context

Shravan's teaching on growth in the MPM cohort deliberately breaks from the conventional AARRR funnel model that dominates PM education. Across Week 5 Session 2 and Week 7 Session 1, he builds a case that AARRR is a useful taxonomy but a dangerous strategy -- it is linear, non-compounding, and has an inefficient cost of distribution over time. Growth loops, by contrast, compound. This mirrors his own experience: Rethink Systems and the Swag Wala PM channel grew through compounding loops, not paid acquisition funnels.

## Details

The core argument begins with a critique of AARRR as a growth model. Shravan acknowledges its value as a framework for understanding user lifecycle stages -- acquisition, activation, retention, referral, revenue -- but argues that as a growth strategy, it is fundamentally limited. AARRR is linear: you pour users in at the top and some fraction survives each stage. There is no defensibility inherent in the model. If someone discovers a profitable niche using AARRR, every competitor can replicate the same funnel. The cost of distribution rises over time because you are competing for the same finite pool of attention through the same channels.

Growth loops solve this by building inbound mechanisms directly into the product that increase frequency of usage. The key distinction Shravan draws is between externally incentivized referrals (which he explicitly says are not loops) and organically driven behavioral cycles where one user action naturally produces another user action by design. Quora is his canonical example: you search for an answer, you find value, you write an answer, someone else searches for your answer -- the two actions (consuming and creating) form a self-reinforcing cycle. LinkedIn operates similarly: reading content inspires writing content, which inspires reading, and the social capital earned from engagement provides intrinsic motivation to continue.

The LinkedIn case study is one of Shravan's most original teaching moments. He asks the class a deceptively simple question: why does LinkedIn index profiles on Google but not content? Students guess authentication, trust, credibility. He walks them through the real answer systematically. LinkedIn's business model depends on time spent inside the platform because that is what advertisers pay for. Profiles are indexed on Google because they serve as an acquisition hook -- you see someone's profile snippet, you click through, you create an account or log in. But if LinkedIn indexed content on Google, users could get answers to their questions without ever entering the platform, which would destroy time-spent metrics and ad revenue. Quora does the opposite because there is no intrinsic value to a Quora profile -- content is all they have to offer. This single example teaches acquisition strategy, monetization alignment, platform dynamics, and competitive analysis simultaneously.

Shravan introduces a distinction between micro loops and macro loops using a system-of-systems diagram. Within a company, individual subsystems form small loops: product and tech teams exchange talent (PMs become TPMs, engineers become PMs), sales and operations reinforce each other. These are micro loops -- not individually powerful, but collectively meaningful. The macro loop emerges when these subsystems interact: product and tech build something, operations and sales sell it, customer feedback flows back to product and tech, they build better, the cycle accelerates. He illustrates this with his own Art and Product Musings community, where users brought users (micro loop 1), creators brought creators (micro loop 2), and users became creators after seeing others write (micro loop 3, the cross-loop that made it a macro loop).

He extends the loop concept into organizational culture with a characteristically sharp observation: toxic workplaces are also loops. A bad actor influences a node, that node influences another, and eventually every new hire falls into the same behavioral structure. "Insanity is not the virtue of the individual. It is the virtue of mobs." Amazon has a driven culture because the loop reinforces that behavior. Microsoft has a calmer culture because its loops reinforce that instead. Culture builds because of loops forming within the company.

The concept of defensible growth ties everything together. Defensible growth is growth that builds its own defense -- social networks are the clearest example, because their value increases with scale in ways that are extremely expensive for competitors to replicate. He contrasts this with channel-product-model thinking: understanding where users come from (SEO, paid, social), what type of product they interact with (marketplace, SaaS, B2B), and what business model captures value (free, freemium, pay-first). The freemium model gets a careful treatment: he distinguishes between "full product for limited time" and "limited features forever," noting that each creates different growth dynamics.

Not all loops are equal, he teaches, using HubSpot versus Looker as a contrast. HubSpot grew fast because its users are marketers whose core job is acquisition -- they naturally create and distribute content, which feeds the loop. A tool built for backend engineers would not have the same loop velocity because engineers' core work is building, not distributing. The audience of the loop determines its power.

## Impact

This framework gives Shravan's students a vocabulary and mental model that goes beyond "optimize the funnel." By teaching them to see products as systems of loops rather than linear funnels, he equips them to design growth into products from the ground up rather than bolting it on after launch. The LinkedIn case study has become one of the most-discussed teaching moments across cohorts -- it demonstrates the analytical depth he expects from graduates. The micro/macro loop framework directly connects to his systems thinking philosophy, reinforcing his belief that everything is a system and the builder who understands systems builds things that last.
