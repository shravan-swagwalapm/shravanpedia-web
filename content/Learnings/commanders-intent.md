> "No plan succeeds when it meets the enemy." The goal is the intent, not the plan.

## Context

Commander's Intent is one of the first frameworks Shravan introduces to every new MPM cohort, and the placement is not accidental. He opens Week 1 with it, before any product management content, because it encodes the single most important lesson he wants students to internalize before they learn a single framework or build a single product: plans will fail, but intent endures. The framework originates from military doctrine, but Shravan reframes it as a product management and life principle that touches everything from roadmapping to career planning to how you respond when the interview process throws an unexpected curveball in round seven.

## Details

### The Military Origin

The concept arrives in the very first mindset session, delivered with the urgency of something that must be understood before anything else can be taught. Shravan tells the cohort that there is a famous military principle: no plan survives contact with the enemy. The phrase, attributed to Helmuth von Moltke the Elder, captures a truth that every military strategist and every product manager eventually learns the hard way — that detailed plans, no matter how carefully constructed, are artifacts of a world that no longer exists the moment execution begins.

What this means in practice is that a squad cannot rely on a detailed plan once the battlefield introduces chaos, unexpected variables, and loss of communication. The radio goes silent. The terrain is different from the map. The enemy does something nobody anticipated. In these moments, soldiers who have been given only a plan are lost. Soldiers who understand the intent can improvise.

Military leaders therefore communicate an intent that is deliberately ambiguous — something like "break the will of the enemy" or "secure the hilltop by dawn." The ambiguity is not a flaw in communication; it is the design. A specific plan tells you what to do when conditions match the plan. An intent tells you what to achieve regardless of conditions. The gap between the two is where judgment lives, and developing that judgment is the entire point of the MPM cohort.

### Mapping to Product Management

Shravan maps this directly onto the product management world with a precision that reveals how deeply he has internalized the framework. He tells students their commander's intent for the cohort is to "become really high quality operators." That statement is not measurable. It is not a KPI. It is not a SMART goal. And that is exactly the point. The intent provides a direction that survives the inevitable chaos of real-world execution.

Consider the chaos a PM faces: a manager who changes priorities without explanation, a market that shifts because a competitor launched something unexpected, a team member who leaves mid-sprint, an interview process where the sixth round goes perfectly but the seventh fails because the hiring manager had a bad day. None of these events can be planned for specifically. All of them can be navigated if the intent is clear. "Become a high quality operator" survives all of them because it is not tied to any specific plan — it is the direction that makes every recovery meaningful.

Shravan pushes this further by asking students to formulate their own commander's intent for their career. Not a five-year plan (those are plans, and they will fail). Not a title they want (those are outcomes, and they are not within your control). An intent: the kind of operator you want to be, the kind of problems you want to solve, the kind of impact you want to have. The ambiguity is the feature. It creates the space in which you can pivot, adapt, and recover without losing your sense of direction.

### The Long-Term Vision, Short-Term Plan Framework

In Week 2, Shravan deepens the framework by connecting it to how great product companies operate. He explains that the best builders have a long-term vision but only short-term plans. A hospital founder's vision is "affordable high-quality healthcare," but the plan is three weeks at a time, because the government might revoke the land approval, the lead doctor might take a better offer, a pandemic might strike, or a new regulation might change the economics entirely.

The stock market becomes his metaphor, and it is a powerful one: the long-term trend line is up, but the day-to-day movement is Brownian motion — essentially random. You cannot predict what happens next week. You can predict the general direction over years. The strategy, therefore, is to plan only what you can execute in the immediate term and to tie each small plan back to the larger intent. Every sprint should answer the question: "Does this move us closer to the intent?" If it does, execute. If it does not, re-plan.

This connects directly to why Agile supplanted waterfall in product development. Shravan argues that waterfall only worked in monopolistic markets like Microsoft in the 1990s, where the lack of competition meant the market would wait for you to finish your eight-month plan. The moment competition existed and markets became dynamic, the waterfall approach of planning for eight months and then shipping became a recipe for irrelevance. Agile's sprint cycles — three to four weeks maximum — are the product management equivalent of commander's intent: know where you want to go, but only plan the next sprint, because the next sprint is the only unit of time you can meaningfully control.

### The NVIDIA Illustration

Shravan uses the NVIDIA story as his most powerful illustration of commander's intent in action, and the story is worth telling in full because it makes the concept visceral.

Jensen Huang started NVIDIA in the 1990s focused on a single intent: reduce the cost of compute through accelerated computing. There was no market for it. Nobody was asking for GPUs to do general computation. For twenty years, gaming was the only segment that wanted what NVIDIA built — the only customer for a technology that Huang believed would eventually change the world. Three times the company nearly could not make payroll. Three times the plans failed. But the intent — reduce the cost of compute — never wavered.

In 2017, OpenAI needed cheap compute to run transformer models at scale. The compute had to be massively parallel, incredibly fast, and available in quantities that traditional CPUs could not provide. NVIDIA was the only company positioned to deliver, because for twenty-five years they had been building toward exactly this moment without knowing when it would come.

The short-term plans changed constantly. Gaming GPUs. Professional visualization. Automotive computing. Data center acceleration. Each plan was a short-term bet tied to the long-term intent. When one plan failed or plateaued, the next one took its place. But the intent — reduce the cost of compute — was unwavering for twenty-five years. The result is a company that went from near-bankruptcy to becoming one of the most valuable companies in the world, not because it had a better plan than its competitors but because it had a clearer intent and the discipline to keep building toward it through every failure, every pivot, and every moment of doubt.

### Commander's Intent and Resilience

The framework has a direct connection to resilience that Shravan makes explicit. When a specific plan fails, the person who was attached to the plan experiences the failure as a catastrophe — the thing they were working toward is gone. But the person who holds the intent experiences the same failure as information — data about which plans work and which do not, data that makes the next plan better.

This reframing is itself a product of the [[life-philosophy]] and [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]] influences in Shravan's thinking. The Gita's nishkama karma — action without attachment to results — is commander's intent at the spiritual level. You perform your duty (execute the plan) without attachment to the outcome (the plan's success or failure) because the intent (your dharma) transcends any single plan. When the interview goes wrong, when the feature flops, when the market shifts — the person rooted in intent recovers faster because they were never attached to the specific plan in the first place.

### Teaching Method: Why This Comes First

The pedagogical choice to open with Commander's Intent rather than with any PM framework is itself a statement about what matters most. Shravan knows that students arrive expecting frameworks, templates, and tools. They expect to learn how to write a PRD, how to run a sprint, how to conduct user research. And they will learn all of that. But first, they need to understand that every framework, template, and tool is a plan — and plans fail. What survives is the intent behind the plan. If the intent is clear, you can rebuild the plan. If the intent is missing, no plan can save you.

This is why the cohort's case studies are deliberately unsolvable, why the Swiggy menu-selector exercise has no right answer, and why the circle exercise produces wildly divergent results. The intent behind these exercises is not to produce correct answers but to develop the judgment required to navigate ambiguity. Commander's intent is not just a framework Shravan teaches. It is the framework that governs how he teaches.

## Impact

Commander's Intent is the connective tissue of the entire MPM curriculum. It frames why product-market fit is dynamic (the market is a changing system, so the "lock" keeps changing shape and the "key" must adapt). It frames why shipping fast matters (short-term plans tied to long-term intent let you iterate toward the vision). It frames why resilience matters more than intelligence (the plan will fail; the question is whether you hold the intent when it does). And it frames why Shravan structures the cohort the way he does: the case studies are deliberately unsolvable, the exercises are deliberately ambiguous, and the intent behind all of it is to produce operators who can navigate chaos with clarity — the same skill a soldier needs on a battlefield and a PM needs in a fast-moving market.
