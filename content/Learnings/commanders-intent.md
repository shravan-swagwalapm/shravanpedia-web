> "No plan succeeds when it meets the enemy." The goal is the intent, not the plan.

## Context

Commander's Intent is one of the first frameworks Shravan introduces to every new MPM cohort. He opens Week 1 with it, before any product management content, because it encodes the single most important lesson he wants students to internalize: plans will fail, but intent endures. The framework originates from military doctrine, but Shravan reframes it as a product management and life principle. He returns to it repeatedly across weeks, connecting it to product-market fit, Agile methodology, roadmapping, and even personal career planning.

## Details

The concept arrives in the very first mindset session. Shravan tells the cohort that there is a famous military principle: no plan survives contact with the enemy. What this means in practice is that a squad cannot rely on a detailed plan once the battlefield introduces chaos, unexpected variables, and loss of communication. Instead, military leaders communicate an intent that is deliberately ambiguous — something like "break the will of the enemy" or "secure the hilltop by dawn." The ambiguity is not a flaw; it is the design. Soldiers who understand the intent can improvise when their specific plan falls apart.

Shravan maps this directly onto the product management world. He tells students their commander's intent for the cohort is to "become really high quality operators." That statement is not measurable. It is not a KPI. And that is exactly the point. The intent provides a direction that survives the inevitable chaos of real-world execution — a manager who changes priorities, a market that shifts, an interview where the sixth round goes perfectly but the seventh fails because the hiring manager had a bad day.

In Week 2, Shravan deepens the framework by connecting it to how great product companies operate. He explains that good builders have a long-term vision but only short-term plans. A hospital founder's vision is "affordable high-quality healthcare," but the plan is three weeks at a time, because the government might revoke the land approval, the lead doctor might take a better offer, or a pandemic might strike. The stock market becomes his metaphor: the long-term trend line is up, but the day-to-day movement is Brownian motion. You cannot predict what happens next week, so you plan only what you can execute now, and you tie each small plan back to the larger intent.

This connects directly to why Agile supplanted waterfall in product development. Shravan argues that waterfall only worked in monopolistic markets like Microsoft in the 1990s, where the lack of competition meant the market would wait for you. The moment competition existed and markets became dynamic, the waterfall approach of planning for eight months and then shipping became a recipe for irrelevance. Agile's sprint cycles — three to four weeks maximum — are the product management equivalent of commander's intent: know where you want to go, but only plan the next sprint.

Shravan uses the NVIDIA story as his most powerful illustration. Jensen Huang started NVIDIA in the 1990s focused on accelerated computing. There was no market for it. For twenty years, gaming was the only segment. Three times the company nearly could not make payroll. But Huang understood the intent — reduce the cost of compute — and when OpenAI needed cheap compute in 2017 to run transformer models at scale, NVIDIA was the only company positioned to deliver. The short-term plans changed constantly, but the commander's intent was unwavering for twenty-five years.

## Impact

Commander's Intent is the connective tissue of the entire MPM curriculum. It frames why product-market fit is dynamic (the market is a changing system, so the "lock" keeps changing shape and the "key" must adapt). It frames why shipping fast matters (short-term plans tied to long-term intent). It frames why resilience matters more than intelligence (the plan will fail; the question is whether you hold the intent). And it frames why Shravan structures the cohort the way he does: the case studies are deliberately unsolvable, because the point is not the solution but the ability to navigate ambiguity with a clear intent.
