## Context

Growth and distribution is Shravan's second most posted-about topic -- 883 posts. But unlike most growth content on LinkedIn, his is not about hacks. It is about understanding systems: how attention flows, how narratives form, why some products grow and others die. He studies TikTok's sorting hat algorithm, Truecaller's 300M-user growth machine, SHEIN's commerce flywheel, and Superhuman's PMF engine -- not for trivia, but to teach his students how to think about distribution as a first-class product concern.

## Details

### Distribution as the Moat

"Media favors those who have strong distribution." This is a recurring conviction that Shravan applies broadly -- to Elon Musk surviving near-bankruptcy, to Adani bouncing back from Hindenburg, to why motivational influencers get amplified regardless of truth. The PM lesson he draws from these cases is precise: "If your decision on a product is shaped by HIPPO, consensus, or anecdote, it almost always ends in the demise of the product." Distribution is not a marketing function. It is a product decision, and the product team that treats it as someone else's problem has already lost.

### The Reading List as Growth Engine

Shravan's most reposted content format is curated reading lists -- articles he recommends to learn business, tech, product, and psychology. These lists function as his own growth engine: they get bookmarked, shared, and re-posted, compounding his reach with every cycle. The articles he recommends most repeatedly reveal his mental models for growth. "TikTok and the Sorting Hat" by Eugene Wei explains how algorithmic distribution beats social graphs. "How Superhuman Built an Engine to Find Product/Market Fit" introduces the 40% "very disappointed" framework. "Truecaller's Growth Machine: Reaching 300M Users" maps network effects in the Indian context. "SHEIN: The TikTok of Commerce" demonstrates supply chain as a growth moat. "Google's Approach: How to Grow to 100 Million Users" teaches systematic scaling. And "Your Life is Driven by Network Effects" extends compounding to careers.

### Content-as-Distribution

Shravan's own growth story is the best case study for his philosophy. His 2023 year recap tells it plainly: 45M+ LinkedIn views, 120K+ followers, 1.5M YouTube views in 2 months, lectures at NTU Singapore, IIT Madras, IIT Kharagpur, IIT Roorkee, and 10+ product conferences. This was not luck and it was not a single viral moment. It was compounding from daily posting since April 2020.

The flywheel works like this: free YouTube content builds trust, LinkedIn posts drive discovery, trust converts to [[rethink-systems]] cohort applications (less than 10% acceptance rate), graduates become mentors and evangelists who amplify the cycle. "The videos have helped more than 200+ PMs transition free of cost and have garnered love of more than 5 lac+ YouTube views." Every layer feeds the next, and the zero-CAC first batch ($150K+ revenue) is proof that content-as-distribution works when the content is genuinely useful, not when it is optimized for engagement.

### On Objectivity in Growth Decisions

"Most good product people are systems thinkers where they believe -- it's not the person who is wrong, it's the system that is, and they make efforts to change that. You change the system, you change the node's behavior." Shravan pushes back hard against consensus-driven product decisions, and his framing is characteristically blunt: "Objectivity in the face of volume is often not considered or thought of -- because 100 people saying a THING can't be wrong, right? Lol -- Hitler also said so, and the Nazis believed so."

The point is not shock value. The point is that growth decisions made by committee tend to converge on the obvious, and the obvious is rarely differentiated. The PM's job is to see what the data shows, not what the crowd believes.

### Psychology of Growth

Shravan's curated reading lists always include psychology alongside product articles, because in his view they are the same discipline applied at different scales. The mental models he teaches recurrently include "The Science of How Customers Buy Anything" on decision architecture, "The Scientists Who Make Apps Addictive" on behavioral design ethics, "If You Run a Small Business, Park in the Back of the Parking Lot" on customer-first thinking, and "Seeing Like an Algorithm" by Eugene Wei on understanding recommendation systems from the inside.

### Community as Distribution

The communities Shravan has built are not just engagement metrics -- they are distribution channels. The #artandproductmusings community scaled to 3,000+ active members in 2 months with 100+ people writing daily. The WhatsApp community hit 1,700+ members. The [[rethink-systems]] alumni network -- 400+ operators from Google, Microsoft, Swiggy, 1mg, Meesho, and others -- is itself a distribution channel where alumni mentor, hire, and open doors for each other. The community is not a byproduct of growth. It is the growth mechanism.

### Quibi as the Anti-Pattern

Shravan repeatedly shares one cautionary tale: "Quibi Is What Happens When Hollywood Overvalues Content And Undervalues Community." This is his core growth lesson distilled to a single case. Content without community is just cost. Distribution without engagement is vanity. Products that win build both, and the ones that build only content -- no matter how much money they pour into it -- eventually discover that distribution cannot be bought. It has to be earned through trust, and trust is earned through community.

## Impact

Shravan's growth philosophy shapes everything he builds. [[rethink-systems]] does not run ads -- it grows through content, community, and results. [[the-swagwala-pm]] became the largest individual PM channel in India through consistency, not virality. The lesson he teaches his students and lives himself: distribution is not a launch-day activity, it is a daily practice that compounds. "Please be responsible and be objective in the face of adversity -- one of the few traits of a PM not spoken of, and yet the most important one."
