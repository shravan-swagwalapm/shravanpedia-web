## Context

Teaching is not a side activity for Shravan -- it is the constant across every career phase. Even at Flipkart, his first job, the instinct was to explain, to mentor, to make complex things accessible. This article captures the teaching arc specifically, separate from the projects it lives inside.

### Curriculum Philosophy

The curriculum blends Bhagavad Gita wisdom with modern product execution -- not as decoration or cultural flavoring, but as foundational framework for how to think about purpose, duty, and detachment from outcomes. Delivery is in Hinglish for accessibility to the Indian audience. The [[builders-bible]] serves as the free, comprehensive text at 700+ pages and always growing.

### Cohort Size Philosophy

Shravan holds a hard conviction on cohort sizing: "You cannot get quality education in any cohort greater than 25 people." The cap is non-negotiable, which creates a natural tension with growth ambitions. His solution is a **gurukul-inspired scaling model**: develop 50 mentors who each train 20 educators, scaling through people rather than class size. The quality stays high because the unit of scale is a trained human, not a larger classroom.

### Notable Exercises and Teaching Methods from the Session Notes

The MPM session notes reveal specific exercises that demonstrate the depth of Shravan's pedagogical design. In Week 3, the case study is a real national problem: eliminating unwanted spam calls and SMS in India with AI-powered smart call filtering. Six teams present creative solutions — Roco (AI-powered spam detection with a central spam registry and SIBIL-like scoring), Unnati Kavach (personalized spam firewall with dramatic skit-style storytelling), Kavach AI (spam call defense using Mahabharata characters Arjun and Draupadi), SpamDefender.ai (real-time AI bodyguard with telco integration), a voice and SMS filter using supervised learning and Gmail-style spam classification, and DTM with fraud insurance (emergency call record triggers and B2B telco alert systems). Shravan's feedback criteria reveal his evaluation priorities: he praises unique insights (recognizing that spam leads to fraud, not just annoyance), real user research (surveys and interviews conducted by students), storytelling energy, and UI prototyping using tools like Lovable.so and Replit. He critiques weak visual representation of the problem, excessive fun at the expense of the core message, crossing the strict five-minute time limit, and unclear solutions. The meta-lesson he draws is the concept of the "insight waterfall" — the moment when the interviewer or audience learns something from the presenter that they did not know before.

In Week 5, the SatyaGPT case study on political misinformation in India produces six parallel team presentations. Teams present in formats ranging from standard pitches to Kaun Banega Crorepati-style game shows with characters and quiz statistics, to conversational desi family-style forward chains. The problem — India as the number one country in political misinformation spread, with 950 million internet users and 64 percent exposed to fake news — produces solutions spanning screenshot-based fact-checking, WhatsApp bot verification, on-device AI flagging, and notification-level verification systems. Shravan's feedback differentiates between teams: he names one team's skit presentation as "top 3 of the last three weeks" for its clear narrative and regional language support, while pushing another to go deeper on solution architecture rather than just naming technologies. He encourages the strongest team to "prototype an MVP by Week 8" and shift from idea to real product, embodying his conviction that the program should produce builders, not theorists.

The Week 4 notes also capture the app design breakout exercise where students must design a dating-safety app by separately articulating conceptual design (what elements should exist), information design (how information is structured), and interaction design (what happens on touch). The exercise teaches that clear, layered design articulation is itself a core PM skill — you do not need to be a designer, but you must communicate design intent at each layer.

### Specific Teaching Moments That Define the Method

Several specific teaching moments from the MPM cohort transcripts reveal how Shravan's method works in practice. The LinkedIn indexing question is one of his most-discussed teaching moments across cohorts: he asks students a deceptively simple question -- why does LinkedIn index profiles on Google but not content? -- and walks them through the real answer systematically, demonstrating acquisition strategy, monetization alignment, platform dynamics, and competitive analysis in a single example. Students guess authentication, trust, and credibility. The actual answer is that LinkedIn's ad revenue depends on time spent inside the platform, so indexing content on Google would let users get answers without entering the platform, destroying time-spent metrics. Quora does the opposite because content is all they have to offer. This single example teaches more about growth loops than any textbook chapter. See [[growth-loops-and-defensible-growth]] for the full framework.

In Week 4, the Swiggy menu-selector exercise forces students to diagnose why a new feature reduced average orders from five to three per month. Students must determine whether the problem is conceptual (the feature disrupts the basic flow), visual (it does not look right), or interactional (the mechanics are flawed). The class splits three ways, and Shravan does not resolve the split. The pedagogical intent is precise: if a case study has one right answer, students optimize for finding it. If it has no right answer, students are forced to develop judgment -- the ability to weigh tradeoffs, argue for a position, and change their mind when evidence warrants it. This is the same skill they need in real product roles, where the PM's job is not to be right but to make the best decision possible with incomplete information.

The circle exercise in the visual design session is another signature moment. Shravan shows four overlapping circles of different sizes and colors and asks students to rank them by importance. Results are wildly divergent -- some rank by size, some by color, some by position. One student applies Whoop's ring-tracking color scheme. Shravan reveals the exercise was deliberately ambiguous, its purpose to demonstrate how much interpretive divergence exists in visual design. If trained PM students cannot agree on which circle matters most, how can you expect an end user to click where you want them to? See [[four-layers-of-design]] for the full framework.

### The Bus Stop Story as Teaching in the Wild

One of the most remarkable teaching moments happened outside any classroom or YouTube session. On a bus from Chandigarh to Jammu, Shravan overheard a Punjabi uncle telling his son on the phone: "Koi ni puttar, jo honda hai waheguru ke karam se hi honda hai." The uncle's son had failed a startup interview and was depressed. Shravan introduced himself, shared his YouTube channel, and forgot about it. Days later, the son called. He had been watching the channel, grew in confidence, and cracked his first product role. But the deeper story was the father: "My father used to worry a lot about me. In the last few weeks, he has been keeping fit and laughing, because I told him I am feeling confident day by day. Somehow my confidence grew his confidence."

This story captures something that no NPS score or placement metric can: the second-order effects of teaching. The son got a job, but the father got his health and happiness back. The teaching multiplied through a family relationship that Shravan never saw. He retells this story not as a case for his own channel but as proof that genuine teaching -- even a casual recommendation on a bus -- compounds in ways the teacher never witnesses. It is the Compassion Funnel in its most organic form, and it connects directly to [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]] -- the Gita's teaching that when you help others without expectation, bliss manifests.

### The $5,000 Per Hour Framework

Shravan's approach to teaching includes a meta-lesson about the value of time itself. He assigns his own time an explicit dollar value of $5,000 per hour and encourages students to do the same. This is not bravado -- it is a prioritization framework. If an hour of your time is worth a specific amount, you structure your day to spend it on things that justify that value. He connects this to why the MPM cohort costs $1,000: the price is not a revenue target but a commitment device. Students who paid treat five-hour sessions differently than they would a free YouTube video. He also uses this framing to explain why the sessions are long: "If I can very well do these lectures in one and a half hours, I can finish it. The problem is you will not develop that depth. You'll not be able to answer at second-order thinking." The length is not indulgence -- it is the time required for behavioral change to take root. ## Impact

Teaching is the multiplier. Every hour Shravan spends teaching feeds the [[content-flywheel]]: it builds the audience (194K+ LinkedIn, 45K+ YouTube), which fills the cohort ($1M+ total revenue, $150K+ from the first batch with zero customer acquisition cost), which funds the building, which generates more to teach. The 10,000+ PMs mentored is the scale proof. The 300+ placed into PM roles is the conversion proof. The bus stop story is the second-order proof -- teaching that compounds through family relationships the teacher never sees.

The recommendations from students confirm that his teaching style -- practical, narrative, first-hand, Socratic, and deliberately long -- is what differentiates him from textbook PMs. The NSS volunteering at IIT Roorkee, at ages 18-22, proves this is intrinsic, not a growth hack. The guest lectures at IIMs, IITs, ISB, and NTU prove it scales beyond YouTube. And the Batch 6 testimonial -- "you have duped us into learning human values instead of product management" -- proves it works at a level that most education programs never reach: behavioral transformation disguised as professional training.
