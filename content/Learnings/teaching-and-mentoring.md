> From IIT classrooms to NTU Singapore -- the thread that runs through every role.

## Context

Teaching is not a side activity for Shravan — it is the constant across every career phase, the thread that connects the IIT Roorkee student who volunteered to teach math to the Principal PM at BYJU'S who started a YouTube channel to the founder of a million-dollar education company. Even at Flipkart, his first job, the instinct was to explain, to mentor, to make complex things accessible. The YouTube channel, the cohort programs, the [[builders-bible]], the guest lectures at institutions across India and internationally — they are all expressions of the same impulse. This impulse is not strategic. It is not a growth hack. It is intrinsic — as natural to Shravan as breathing — and the evidence for this starts at age 18, long before product management was even a phrase in his vocabulary.

## Details

### The NSS Origin: Teaching Before Teaching Was the Plan

At **NSS at IIT Roorkee** (2011-2015), Shravan taught math to underprivileged students in nearby villages. This is the earliest evidence of the teaching drive — a college student who, given free time and no obligation, chose to spend it making mathematics accessible to children who had no access to quality education. The choice is significant because it predates everything that followed: the PM career, the YouTube channel, the cohort programs, the company. It reveals that teaching is not something Shravan does because it is profitable or because it builds his brand. He does it because it is who he is.

The experience also planted the seed for the [[hinglish-teaching]] approach that would later define @TheSwagWalaPM. Teaching math to students in rural Uttarakhand meant meeting them in their language, using their references, finding the explanation that made sense to them rather than the one that was academically correct. This is the same pedagogical instinct that, a decade later, would produce YouTube videos that explain PM concepts using Bollywood references, Age of Empires metaphors, and Hinglish that makes complex ideas feel like conversations with an older brother.

### Community Teaching and the Cohort Model

The [[rethink-systems]] cohort, "Mastering Product Management 2.0," runs as an 8-week live CBC with 100 participants at $1,000 per seat, combining AI-first execution with design thinking and real-world building. The 8th cohort launches September 27, 2026. His **Claude Code workshops** — "Learn 90% of Claude Code in 90 minutes" — teach non-developers to build production software, extending the builder philosophy beyond PMs into a broader audience of operators who see what AI makes possible.

The cohort model is not just an educational choice — it is a pedagogical conviction. Sessions run five hours or longer because Shravan believes that behavioral transformation requires sustained immersion. "If I can very well do these lectures in one and a half hours, I can finish it. The problem is you will not develop that depth. You'll not be able to answer at second-order thinking." The length is not indulgence — it is the time required for new thinking patterns to take root.

### Curriculum Philosophy

The curriculum blends Bhagavad Gita wisdom with modern product execution — not as decoration or cultural flavoring, but as foundational framework for how to think about purpose, duty, and detachment from outcomes. The [[three-superpowers]] — gratitude, unlearning, and compassion — are installed in Week 1 before any product content begins. By Week 7, students discover that the entire curriculum was teaching [[systems-thinking]] in disguise. The final lecture of Batch 6 ran 7.5 hours and produced the statement that defined the program: "This is not a product management program but a program deeply rooted in human values."

Delivery is in Hinglish for accessibility to the Indian audience. The [[builders-bible]] serves as the free, comprehensive text at 700+ pages and always growing. The combination of these elements — spiritual depth, first-principles thinking, Hinglish delivery, free resources, and selective cohorts — creates a learning experience that no competing program replicates.

### Cohort Size Philosophy

Shravan holds a hard conviction on cohort sizing: "You cannot get quality education in any cohort greater than 25 people." The cap is non-negotiable, which creates a natural tension with growth ambitions. His solution is a **gurukul-inspired scaling model**: develop 50 mentors who each train 20 educators, scaling through people rather than class size. The quality stays high because the unit of scale is a trained human, not a larger classroom. This model traces directly back to the NSS experience — the kid who taught math in villages understood intuitively that education requires a human connection that cannot be replicated by scaling class sizes.

### Notable Exercises and Teaching Methods

The MPM session notes reveal specific exercises that demonstrate the pedagogical sophistication beneath the casual Hinglish delivery.

In Week 3, the case study is a real national problem: eliminating unwanted spam calls and SMS in India with AI-powered smart call filtering. Six teams present creative solutions — Roco (AI-powered spam detection with a central spam registry), Unnati Kavach (personalized spam firewall with dramatic skit-style storytelling), Kavach AI (spam call defense using Mahabharata characters Arjun and Draupadi), SpamDefender.ai (real-time AI bodyguard with telco integration), a voice and SMS filter using supervised learning, and DTM with fraud insurance. Shravan's feedback criteria reveal his evaluation priorities: he praises unique insights (recognizing that spam leads to fraud, not just annoyance), real user research (surveys and interviews conducted by students), storytelling energy, and UI prototyping using tools like Lovable.so and Replit. He critiques weak visual representation, excessive fun at the expense of the core message, crossing the strict five-minute time limit, and unclear solutions. The meta-lesson he draws is the "insight waterfall" — the moment when the audience learns something they did not know before.

In Week 5, the SatyaGPT case study on political misinformation in India produces six parallel team presentations in formats ranging from standard pitches to Kaun Banega Crorepati-style game shows. The problem — India as the number one country in political misinformation spread, with 950 million internet users and 64 percent exposed to fake news — produces solutions spanning screenshot-based fact-checking, WhatsApp bot verification, on-device AI flagging, and notification-level verification systems. Shravan differentiates between teams: he names one team's skit presentation as "top 3 of the last three weeks" and pushes the strongest team to "prototype an MVP by Week 8" — embodying his conviction that the program should produce builders, not theorists.

The Week 4 design exercise asks students to design a dating-safety app by separately articulating conceptual design, information design, and interaction design. The forced separation teaches that clear, layered design articulation is itself a core PM skill. See [[four-layers-of-design]] for the full framework.

### Specific Teaching Moments That Define the Method

The LinkedIn indexing question is one of Shravan's most-discussed teaching moments across cohorts. He asks a deceptively simple question: why does LinkedIn index profiles on Google but not content? Students guess authentication, trust, credibility. The actual answer is that LinkedIn's ad revenue depends on time spent inside the platform, so indexing content on Google would let users get answers without entering the platform, destroying time-spent metrics. Quora does the opposite because content is all they have to offer. This single example teaches acquisition strategy, monetization alignment, platform dynamics, and competitive analysis simultaneously. See [[growth-loops-and-defensible-growth]] for the full framework.

The Swiggy menu-selector exercise forces students to diagnose why a new feature reduced average orders from five to three per month. The class splits three ways on whether the problem is conceptual, visual, or interactional, and Shravan does not resolve the split. The pedagogical intent is precise: if a case study has one right answer, students optimize for finding it. If it has no right answer, students develop judgment — the ability to weigh tradeoffs, argue for a position, and change their mind when evidence warrants it. This is the same skill they need in real product roles.

The circle exercise shows four overlapping circles of different sizes and colors and asks students to rank them by importance. Results are wildly divergent. Shravan reveals the exercise was deliberately ambiguous — its purpose is to demonstrate how much interpretive divergence exists in visual design. If trained PM students cannot agree on which circle matters most, the designer must make the hierarchy unmistakable. See [[four-layers-of-design]] for the full framework.

### The Bus Stop Story as Teaching in the Wild

One of the most remarkable teaching moments happened outside any classroom or YouTube session. On a bus from Chandigarh to Jammu, Shravan overheard a Punjabi uncle telling his son on the phone: "Koi ni puttar, jo honda hai waheguru ke karam se hi honda hai." The uncle's son had been struggling after a startup interview. Shravan introduced himself, shared his YouTube channel, and forgot about it.

Days later, the son called. He had been watching the channel, grown in confidence, and cracked his first product role. But the deeper story was the father: "My father used to worry a lot about me. In the last few weeks, he has been keeping fit and laughing, because I told him I am feeling confident day by day. Somehow my confidence grew his confidence."

This story captures something that no NPS score or placement metric can: the second-order effects of teaching. The son got a job, but the father got his health and happiness back. The teaching multiplied through a family relationship that Shravan never saw. He retells this story not as a case for his own channel but as proof that genuine teaching — even a casual recommendation on a bus — compounds in ways the teacher never witnesses. It is the Compassion Funnel in its most organic form, and it connects directly to [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]]: the Gita's teaching that when you help others without expectation, bliss manifests.

### The $5,000 Per Hour Framework

Shravan assigns his own time an explicit dollar value of $5,000 per hour and encourages students to do the same. This is not bravado — it is a prioritization framework. If an hour of your time is worth a specific amount, you structure your day to spend it on things that justify that value. He connects this to why the MPM cohort costs $1,000: the price is not a revenue target but a commitment device. Students who paid treat five-hour sessions differently than they would a free YouTube video.

He also uses this framing to explain why the sessions are long: "If I can very well do these lectures in one and a half hours, I can finish it. The problem is you will not develop that depth. You'll not be able to answer at second-order thinking." The length is not indulgence — it is the time required for behavioral change to take root, for new patterns of thinking to overwrite old ones. See [[mpm-cohort-pedagogy]] for the full pedagogical architecture.

### Three Pillars of Growth

From his Medium and Socialboat writing, three pillars underpin professional growth: **passion** as the prerequisite for everything — you cannot sustain the consistency required without genuine love for the work; **prioritization** as the discipline to focus on what matters most rather than what feels most urgent; and **feedback receptiveness** as the willingness to hear and act on criticism rather than defending against it. He adds a corollary that reveals his spiritual orientation: "People unconcerned with rewards typically achieve ten times more success." The corollary is nishkama karma in professional language — the Gita's instruction that detachment from outcomes paradoxically produces better outcomes.

## Impact

Teaching is the multiplier. Every hour Shravan spends teaching feeds the [[content-flywheel]]: it builds the audience (194K+ LinkedIn, 45K+ YouTube), which fills the cohort ($1M+ total revenue, $150K+ from the first batch with zero customer acquisition cost), which funds the building, which generates more to teach. The 10,000+ PMs mentored is the scale proof. The 300+ placed into PM roles is the conversion proof. The bus stop story is the second-order proof — teaching that compounds through family relationships the teacher never sees.

The recommendations from students confirm that his teaching style — practical, narrative, first-hand, Socratic, and deliberately long — is what differentiates him from textbook PMs. The NSS volunteering at IIT Roorkee, at ages 18-22, proves this is intrinsic, not a growth hack. The guest lectures at IIMs, IITs, ISB, and NTU prove it scales beyond YouTube. And the Batch 6 testimonial — "you have duped us into learning human values instead of product management" — proves it works at a level that most education programs never reach: behavioral transformation disguised as professional training.
