> Resumes don't work anymore. You have to build multiple pathways. One might work.

## Context

Personal branding is not a standalone lecture topic in Shravan's MPM curriculum -- it is woven into nearly every session from Week 5 onward, with increasing urgency as the cohort approaches its final weeks. Shravan treats it as the connective tissue between learning and outcomes: you can master analytics, design, and systems thinking, but if you have no public presence and no network, those skills remain invisible when it matters most. This comes from lived experience. His own career inflection points -- the 100% hike, the YouTube channel growth, the cohort's organic enrollment -- all came from making his work visible, not from sending resumes.

## Details

The core teaching begins with a blunt diagnosis: the resume-first approach to career advancement is dead, especially in the AI age. Shravan tells his students that writing on LinkedIn is not optional. He does not frame this as a content strategy or a growth hack; he frames it as relationship infrastructure. When he invites guest speakers to the cohort -- people like Dilip (17 years at Microsoft, now Global Partner and Alliance Leader at Adobe), Mayur (VP-level), and Pawan (VP of Freight Tiger) -- he pushes students to write publicly about what they learned from those guests. Not for his benefit ("What is my incentive? My brand is already big enough.") but because the act of writing creates a relationship that a LinkedIn connection request never will. When a guest sees that someone thoughtfully summarized their talk, they remember that person. That memory converts into referrals, conversations, and eventually opportunities.

His teaching on asking good questions is a personal branding lesson disguised as career advice. He describes receiving 50-60 DMs daily on LinkedIn, of which 45 are templated requests: review my resume, become my mentor, refer me. He responds to none of those. What he responds to is someone who says: "I saw this video of yours. On the 22nd minute, you talked about this principle. I have a contextual question." That message demonstrates three things -- the person did their discovery, invested time, and is not wasting his. For guests visiting the cohort, the same dynamic applies. A generic "how do you design products?" is forgettable. "You worked at OLX -- I saw the app and noticed a lot of clutter in the interface. Why is it designed that way?" is a question that creates a lasting impression.

The network effect of writing compounds over time, and Shravan is explicit about the math. If he likes a student's post, 50 more people see it. Those 50 impressions are free distribution that the student could not buy. Over 20 weeks of consistent writing, even twice a week, the cumulative effect becomes visible. He cites former students like Kevin, Ashu, and Tushar who started writing during their cohort and saw tangible career results. He also cites the example of a VP of Sales at CleverTap turned Chief Growth Officer at ClueTrack who writes consistently -- not because he needs to, but because networks are built this way.

The concept of building multiple pathways is central. Shravan explicitly tells students not to rely on a single channel for career advancement. Write on LinkedIn, do podcast appearances, engage with guests, build a portfolio, ask for recommendations -- any one of these might be the pathway that opens a specific opportunity. He frames this as true at every career level: it works for APMs, it works for GPMs, it works for directors of product, it works for founders. The IIT Delhi lecture story illustrates this: every senior person in the room (the CFO of Zepto, the founder of Clovia) had researched everyone else before they met. Discovery is the norm at every level.

He is pragmatic about the fear of writing publicly. "People still abuse me for one reason or the other." His response is to focus on the two people who like the content rather than the five who do not. "Come in abundance mindset. When you show gratitude to other people, this is where opportunities come." He connects this directly to the QnA sessions where students ask about handling negative feedback -- his answer is always the same: your journey is yours, comparison should only be with yourself, and consistent action over time is what compounds.

The storytelling skill gets explicit attention in the QnA sessions. When a student (Mansukh) asks how to become a product management consultant, Shravan identifies two foundational skills: structured thinking and storytelling. "The most powerful person in this world is the storyteller." He illustrates with the Martin Luther King "I Have a Dream" speech and then with a personal anecdote about meeting a CMO from Spotify India. The CMO gave functional specifications for an app but had not thought about narrative specifications -- what message the product should convey. Shravan's ability to name that gap created immediate trust. Storytelling, in his framework, is not presentation polish; it is the ability to name what matters in a way that moves people to action.

His advice to a tier-3 college student named Tanishq encapsulates his entire personal branding philosophy. He tells Tanishq to stop thinking about the disadvantage of his college and start building proof: make a product, grow a distribution channel, write consistently, go to conferences, do debating. He cites a VIT student who took all of Shravan's LinkedIn content, fed it into an LLM, created a course, sold it on Gumroad, and made $2,000 -- then gave Shravan $400 of it. "Why won't that person take an APM role fight tomorrow?" The brand is not the credential; the brand is the evidence of what you have built.

## Impact

Personal branding is where Shravan's teaching philosophy most clearly mirrors his own career arc. The Swag Wala PM channel, the LinkedIn presence at 194K+ followers, the Rethink Systems enrollment pipeline -- all were built through the exact practices he teaches: write consistently, engage with generosity, make work visible, let results compound. By embedding this into the cohort rather than treating it as an optional workshop, he ensures that students leave not just with PM skills but with the distribution infrastructure to make those skills visible to the market. The Tanishq story and the VIT student story are already becoming part of the cohort's mythology, cited by alumni as the moment they understood that their career was something they could actively build rather than passively wait for.
