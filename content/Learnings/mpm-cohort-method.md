## Context

The transcripts from Weeks 5-8 of the MPM cohort reveal something that no marketing page or LinkedIn post captures: the actual mechanics of how Shravan runs his teaching sessions. This is not a lecture series. It is a structured environment that combines Socratic questioning, competitive team presentations, real-time case study feedback, philosophical tangents that turn out to be foundational lessons, and an overt culture of reciprocal accountability. Understanding the method explains why the program consistently produces NPS scores above 90 and why graduates describe being "duped into learning human values instead of product management."

## Details

The eight-week structure follows a deliberate arc. Weeks 1-3 cover principles: where product management originated, problem solving (the golden circle, problem space vs solution space, importance vs satisfaction), and design thinking (first principles, conceptual/information/interaction/visual layers). Weeks 4-5 introduce analytics and experimentation. Week 6 covers roadmaps and prioritization. Week 7 introduces system dynamics as the capstone framework that retroactively explains why everything before it was taught. Week 8 focuses on interviews, personal branding, and preparation for the product-a-thon (the final team competition). Throughout, there are weekly guest speakers from industry and a parallel case study that teams work on continuously.

The case study presentations are the heart of the program's learning model. Teams of 4-6 students receive a problem brief each week and present their solution in a 5-6 minute timed presentation the following week. By Week 6-7, the presentations have evolved into elaborate productions: teams create fictional podcasts (Joe Rogan interviewing Mark Zuckerberg and Elon Musk about deepfakes), Bollywood-inspired skits (Bhuvan Bawra meeting a content creator), news broadcasts (Palki Sharma covering the content creation industry), and comedy shows (India's Got Latent featuring a Bihar village character discussing legal tech). The creativity is not decoration -- it demonstrates that teams have internalized the problem deeply enough to explain it through storytelling.

Shravan's feedback on these presentations reveals his evaluation criteria. He praises solution space depth over surface polish: the team that understood that the core problem in AI video generation is not connecting tools but fixing the prompting layer gets called "by far the best presentation of all four weeks" -- not because of their UI or their slides, but because they identified the architectural bottleneck. He criticizes presentations that use technical jargon without understanding it ("the audience might not be the right audience here to really understand how the architecture actually works"). He rewards presentations that show edge cases ("if you would have shown a little bit around it, that would have been very lovely"). The message is consistent: depth of understanding over breadth of vocabulary.

The Socratic method is constant. In every session captured in the transcripts, Shravan asks questions and waits for students to answer before he shares his perspective. When teaching A/B testing, he asks which form will produce better business outcomes and lets multiple students give conflicting answers before revealing that the question itself is more complex than it appears. When teaching growth loops, he asks why LinkedIn does not index content on Google and lets seven students answer wrong before explaining the ad revenue model. This approach forces active thinking rather than passive consumption.

Guest speakers serve a dual purpose that Shravan makes explicit. The learning value is obvious: a GPM from Adobe with 17 years at Microsoft brings perspectives no textbook can. But the career value is equally important. Shravan tells students that guests will remember them if they ask good questions, that guests will come to him and ask "do you recommend this person," and that writing about guests on LinkedIn creates a relationship that converts into referrals. He names specific alumni outcomes: Pawan, the VP of Freight Tiger, took multiple people from the program. Ravi, his first boss, hired from the cohort. The recommendation economy is not implicit -- it is part of the curriculum.

The philosophical tangents are perhaps the most distinctive feature of the MPM method. A question about LLM courses turns into a five-minute discussion about reading Egyptian mythology in 7th grade and why Shastra Darshan (reading across multiple scriptures) is the model for learning AI. A question about financial freedom turns into a lesson about Adam 1 and Adam 2 and the difference between wealth and wisdom. A student's anxiety about being from a tier-3 college turns into a ten-minute discourse on self-worth, locus of identity, and the compounding effect of consistent action. These are not digressions. They are the delivery mechanism for Shravan's deepest conviction: "You don't need a lot of frameworks to become a better PM, you just have to be a better human to become a better PM."

The weekly book prizes (he gives out physical books like "Beyond Entrepreneurship" to winning teams) and the competitive team structure create a gamification layer that drives engagement. Teams have names (Alpha Crew, Spartans, Prabodh, Amit Jira, Product Ninjas, Sehim Kage, Team Run), mentors, and a leaderboard. By Week 7-8, the competitive energy has transformed into collaborative pride -- teams applaud each other's presentations and replay funny moments.

The scale constraints are intentional. Shravan caps cohorts at approximately 25 students (though later cohorts have grown to 100), because quality interaction degrades at larger sizes. He solves for scale not by increasing class size but through the Gurukul model: train mentors who train educators. The program charges approximately $1,000/seat, which serves as a selection filter -- "you earned this access by paying a certain amount of money, going through a certain kind of structure."

## Impact

The MPM cohort method is Shravan's most complete expression of his teaching philosophy. It demonstrates that product management education done well is not about transferring information (which YouTube and books do fine) but about changing how students think, interact, and present themselves. The progression from principles to systems thinking, the competitive team presentations that force depth, the guest speaker economy that builds networks, and the philosophical interludes that build character -- together they produce what Batch 6 described as "being duped into learning human values instead of product management." That quote, which Shravan returns to repeatedly, is the highest praise the program has received, because it validates his core belief that becoming a better human is the prerequisite for becoming a better PM.
