> Action without attachment to results -- the builder's paradox resolved by an ancient instruction.

## Context

The central tension in building products is that you must care deeply enough to ship excellent work while simultaneously accepting that the market, the timing, the user's mood, and a hundred other variables you cannot control will determine whether that work succeeds. Most builders resolve this tension poorly: either they become mercenary and optimize for metrics at the expense of craft, or they become perfectionists and optimize for craft at the expense of shipping. The Bhagavad Gita's concept of nishkama karma -- desireless action, or more precisely, action performed as duty without attachment to fruit -- resolves this tension at its root.

Shravan encountered this teaching through Srila Prabhupada's Bhagavad Gita As It Is, and it has become the philosophical foundation beneath his [[building-philosophy]], his [[product-management-philosophy]], and his teaching at [[rethink-systems]].

## Details

### The Verse

Bhagavad Gita 2.47 contains the instruction in its purest form: "You have a right to perform your prescribed duty, but you are not entitled to the fruits of action. Never consider yourself the cause of the results of your activities, and never be attached to not doing your duty."

Prabhupada's purport identifies three categories: prescribed duties, capricious work, and inaction. The verse does not instruct passivity. It forbids three things simultaneously: claiming ownership of results, letting anticipated results govern the quality of effort, and using uncertainty as an excuse for inaction. The fourth line -- "never be attached to not doing your duty" -- is as forceful as the prohibition on fruit-attachment. Inaction is not an option.

### The Builder's Paradox

Every serious builder has lived the paradox. You pour months into a product. You ship it. The response is silence, or worse, indifference. The temptation is to conclude that the effort was wasted and to calibrate future effort based on expected reception. This is precisely what the Gita prohibits. The prescribed duty was to build well. The reception is fruit. Calibrating effort to expected reception is making yourself the cause of the results, which produces what Prabhupada calls bondage -- in secular terms, the anxiety-driven cycle where builders only ship what they think will be rewarded.

Shravan's own career demonstrates the alternative. The Builder's Bible reached 198,000 words across 61 files generated by 20+ parallel agents. Nobody asked for it. No market research suggested it would find an audience. The work was done because the work was the duty -- teaching systems thinking to PMs who needed it. [[builders-bible]] shipped because the activity was its own justification, not because the outcome was guaranteed. The same pattern holds for [[prabhupada-ai]], which is the most personally meaningful project in Shravan's portfolio but has the least obvious commercial rationale.

### Action Is Superior to Inaction

Chapter 3 makes the case against inaction with absolute clarity. Verse 3.5 establishes that no one can refrain from doing something even for a moment, because the modes of material nature compel action. Verse 3.8 delivers the instruction: "Perform your prescribed duty, for doing so is better than not working. One cannot even maintain one's physical body without work."

Prabhupada's purport on 3.8 is particularly relevant to builders. He writes that Lord Krishna did not want Arjuna to become a pretender -- someone who poses as having renounced action while actually being paralyzed by indecision. "Work should not be given up capriciously, without purification of materialistic propensities." The instruction is aimed at those who disguise fear of failure as spiritual detachment. In the building world, this disguise takes the form of endless planning, research loops, and strategy documents that never resolve into shipped work.

Shravan's CLAUDE.md rule -- "Do, then report. NEVER narrate intent." -- is this verse translated into an engineering workflow. The prohibition is not against planning but against using planning as a substitute for action.

### Detachment Is Not Indifference

The most common misreading of nishkama karma is that it counsels indifference. It does not. Verse 2.48 instructs: "Perform your duty equipoised, O Arjuna, abandoning all attachment to success or failure. Such equanimity is called yoga." Prabhupada's purport clarifies that Arjuna has nothing to do with the results of the fight because gain or victory are Krishna's concern. This is not indifference to outcomes; it is clarity about jurisdiction. You control the effort. You do not control the result.

In product terms, the distinction maps to leading versus lagging indicators. A PM controls discovery quality, research rigor, specification clarity, and engineering collaboration. These are leading indicators. Revenue, retention, NPS -- these are lagging indicators, produced by the leading indicators plus market conditions, timing, competition, and luck. Attaching self-worth to lagging indicators is fruit-attachment. Attaching effort to leading indicators is prescribed duty.

Shravan's health transformation in 2025 followed the same pattern, as described in [[life-philosophy]]. When his doctor told him his body would die before his startup, his response was not to set a weight target and obsess over the scale. It was to walk 7,000 steps, then 10,000, then 20,000, then 30,000 daily. The action was the action. The result -- 82 kg, 16% body fat, clean medical reports -- followed the action. He did not negotiate with the outcome. He performed the prescribed duty.

### Work as Sacrifice

Verse 3.9 introduces a dimension that separates Gita-informed building from secular productivity philosophy: "Work done as a sacrifice for Visnu has to be performed; otherwise work causes bondage in this material world." Prabhupada's purport states that any work not done for the satisfaction of the Supreme becomes a cause of bondage, whether the work itself appears good or evil.

This is the verse that explains why [[prabhupada-ai]] sits at the top of Shravan's personal project hierarchy despite having less commercial traction than [[predict-my-layoff]] or [[rethink-systems]]. The work is sacrifice -- seva -- offered for the satisfaction of the Lord. In Prabhupada's framework, this orientation transforms the nature of the activity itself, making it transcendental to the reactions of good or evil work. A builder who builds as offering is free from the anxiety cycle, not through willpower but through the redirection of purpose.

### Verse 3.19 and the Attainment of the Supreme

The chapter's practical summary comes in 3.19: "Therefore, without being attached to the fruits of activities, one should act as a matter of duty, for by working without attachment one attains the Supreme." Prabhupada's purport explains that to act on behalf of the Supreme is to act without attachment for the result, and that this is perfect action of the highest degree.

The word "Supreme" here is not a metaphor for excellence or peak performance. Prabhupada means the Personality of Godhead for the devotees, and liberation for the impersonalist. But even read as a secular instruction, the verse resolves the builder's paradox: the way to build the best work of your life is to stop trying to build the best work of your life and instead do your duty as well as you can, without negotiating with the outcome.

## Impact

Nishkama karma is the operating instruction beneath Shravan's most distinctive qualities as a builder: the willingness to start projects without guaranteed outcomes, the patience to teach 200+ PMs at zero cost, the 30K steps per day without obsessing over the scale, the decision to build a spiritual AI app when commercial AI apps would have been easier to monetize. None of these decisions make sense through a purely rational cost-benefit lens. They make sense through 2.47: "You have a right to perform your prescribed duty, but you are not entitled to the fruits of action."
