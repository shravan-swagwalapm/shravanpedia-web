> The gap between a system's complexity and the PM's ability to understand it is where chaos lives.

## Context

In the second week of every MPM cohort, Shravan introduces a framework he draws on the spot using draw.io. He calls it the PM Compute Gap, and it is his explanation for why some product teams succeed spectacularly and others collapse despite ample funding and talent. The framework is not sourced from any textbook — it is Shravan's original synthesis of systems thinking, startup failure modes, and the reason companies like Google and Netflix pay top compensation to product managers. He considers it foundational enough to teach before any tactical PM skills.

## Details

Shravan opens the framework by asking a question: as systems grow more complex, what happens if the product manager's ability to comprehend that complexity does not grow at the same rate? He draws two curves on a shared screen. The first curve represents the complexity of the system — the product, the market, the number of interacting nodes. As a company scales from 100 users to 10,000, the system becomes exponentially more complex. New integrations, new failure modes, new user behaviors, new regulatory requirements.

The second curve represents the PM's "compute" — their ability to abstract, understand, and reason about the system. In the failure scenario, the system complexity grows steeply while the PM's compute grows only marginally. Shravan draws the widening gap between the two curves and labels it: this is chaos. This is where systems fail. The PM cannot see variables that the system has introduced through growth. They run experiments that fail not because the hypothesis was wrong, but because they could not perceive the complexity that made the experiment irrelevant. This is the zone where teams say "we didn't understand the market," "we were too early," or "we couldn't find product-market fit" — but the real problem is that their ability to comprehend the system lagged behind the system's actual complexity.

In the success scenario, Shravan draws the PM's compute curve growing faster than the system's complexity. The PM always has surplus capacity to understand the system, anticipate failure modes, and design solutions that account for second-order effects. He explains that this is why companies like Google and Netflix pay extraordinary compensation to product managers: they need to ensure that the person responsible for the system's purpose has enough cognitive capacity for the kind of complex products they build. He references Reed Hastings' two tenets from "No Rules Rules" — insanely high talent density and radical candor — as the cultural expression of this principle.

Shravan then connects the framework to unsustainable growth. When venture capitalists pour money into a startup and it scales rapidly, processes break because the team's compute never caught up with the complexity that rapid scaling introduced. Growth without corresponding increase in understanding acts like cancer — the nodes in the system are interacting in ways nobody can track.

The framework has a deeply personal dimension for Shravan. He uses it to explain why the cohort is deliberately intensive — five-hour sessions, case studies with no clear answer, forcing students to think at second-order levels. He is not teaching product management content; he is deliberately expanding their compute, so that when they encounter complex systems in their careers, the gap between their understanding and the system's complexity is manageable rather than catastrophic.

## Impact

The PM Compute Gap is one of the most distinctive frameworks in Shravan's teaching arsenal. It gives students a mental model for understanding why smart people fail at product management (their compute did not match the system's complexity), why experience matters (it expands compute over time), and why AI is supplementary rather than threatening (it augments compute but does not replace the structural understanding needed to direct it). It also explains Shravan's own career trajectory: a decade of product leadership was him building compute across multiple domains, and the cohort program is his attempt to compress that compute-building process for others.
