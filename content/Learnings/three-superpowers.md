> Gratitude, unlearning, and compassion — the operating system Shravan installs before any PM content begins.

## Context

Before a single word about product management is spoken in any MPM cohort, Shravan dedicates the entire first session to mindset. He calls it the Mindset Conversation, and at its center are three capabilities he frames as "superpowers" that students must unlock to get anything meaningful from the coming eight weeks. These are not soft-skill platitudes tacked onto a curriculum. They are the prerequisites — the operating system on which all the technical product knowledge will run. He is explicit about this: the first change is behavioral, the second is intellectual. If you skip the behavioral layer, the intellectual layer has nothing to attach to.

## Details

### Superpower One: The Attitude of Gratitude

The first superpower is gratitude, but Shravan defines it operationally, not sentimentally. Gratitude in the cohort means: if you can help someone, you do not ask, you help. You take initiative. If a teammate has a family emergency, you pick up their work without being asked. The goal is to think as one entity, not as individuals competing for a score.

Shravan frames this as a cultural principle, not a nicety. He explains that gratitude is the mechanism by which a community becomes multidirectional — students learn from every node in the network, not just from the instructor at the center. When past cohort members spontaneously congratulate incoming students, that is the ethos of gratitude in action. It is the behavior that transforms a paid program into a family, and he is unapologetic about claiming that distinction: "Today, this large community of operators has become family — not like family, but truly family."

The session notes add a six-stage gratitude evolution framework that maps the journey of self-awareness: No Self in childhood (no self-identity, shaped by environment), Some Self (early achievements define identity — "I'm smart, I'm skilled"), Pride (arrogance sets in, learning stops), Failure (reality check — self-worth is questioned), Gratitude (realization that success was due to others' help), and No Self again at Enlightenment (doing things for joy, without ego). The accompanying quote crystallizes the progression: "The person who blames others is nowhere. The person who blames themselves is halfway there. The person who blames no one has arrived."

### Superpower Two: The Ability to Unlearn

The second superpower is unlearning, and Shravan positions it as harder than learning. Students arrive with biases from years of professional experience — "I've been an engineering manager for 20 years, how can you teach me about APIs?" He draws a distinction between not speaking and active listening. Most people in the room are not speaking. Very few are actively listening, which means hearing the insight between the words, catching what is being communicated beneath the literal content.

He frames unlearning as sequential: first unlearn, then learn, then keep relearning. The people who struggle most in the cohort are not those with the least experience but those with the most rigid priors. There is a large gap between what people think they know and what they actually know, and that gap shows up in work. The builder who says "entrepreneurship is easy, I can do this" from the outside discovers the boring, unglamorous reality only when they enter the details — just as someone critiquing an architect's design discovers the reason every space and joint exists once they try to build it themselves.

### Superpower Three: The Power of Compassion

The third superpower receives the most teaching time, and it is where Shravan's philosophy becomes most distinctive. He deliberately uses the word compassion — not empathy, not kindness, not generosity. Each word was considered and rejected. Compassion, spoken in Sanskrit as Karuna, carries a specific meaning he wants students to internalize.

He opens by asking the class to define the difference between love and compassion, then lets students debate for ten minutes. The answers converge on three properties: compassion is universal (not directed at one person), compassion is rooted in action (not just feeling), and compassion is selfless (without expectation of return). But then Shravan challenges the third property. He asks: is anything truly selfless? Even a mother's love carries some expectation. He pushes the class to confront an uncomfortable truth — pure selflessness does not exist in the way people romanticize it.

His resolution is systems-level: compassion means you believe the world is one system, and anything you do in the interest of the system will eventually benefit you. It is not transactional in the direct sense, but it is rational at the systems level. He uses the analogy of fitness — when you exercise, everything improves, not just one organ. The heart does not ask for payment before pumping blood. He tells the story of a previous organization where nobody wrote documentation. As a junior PM, he started writing PRDs alone. For three months, nobody cared. By the fourth month, the entire team was writing PRDs, because in every meeting he was the only one who was contextual, structured, and prepared. Compassion in action created cultural change.

He then connects this to product management: trust is the only mechanism that builds loyalty and repeat engagement. Manipulation, peer pressure, price discounts, and novelty can drive transactions, but loyalty comes only from trust. And trust is built when you go out of your way to help without calculating the return. This is the Compassion Funnel at a deeper level — not just the progression from compassion to curiosity to passion to critical thinking that he posts on LinkedIn, but the underlying philosophy that compassion is rational when you think at the systems level.

## Impact

The Three Superpowers are not mentioned once and forgotten. They are the cultural fabric of every cohort. When students present case studies in Bollywood-style narratives and help teammates who miss sessions, that is gratitude in operation. When senior professionals allow a 25-year-old mentor to redirect their thinking, that is unlearning in practice. When Unnati, a cohort-one student, helped a friend write a better PRD with no expectation of reward and received a job offer because of it, that is compassion producing outcomes at the systems level. Shravan designed these superpowers to be the invisible curriculum — the thing students discover they learned only after the program ends, when Batch 6 says "you duped us into learning human values instead of product management."
