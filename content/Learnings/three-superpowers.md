> Gratitude, unlearning, and compassion — the operating system Shravan installs before any PM content begins.

## Context

Before a single word about product management is spoken in any MPM cohort, Shravan dedicates the entire first session to mindset. He calls it the Mindset Conversation, and at its center are three capabilities he frames as "superpowers" that students must unlock to get anything meaningful from the coming eight weeks. These are not soft-skill platitudes tacked onto a curriculum to make it feel holistic. They are the prerequisites — the operating system on which all the technical product knowledge will run. He is explicit about this ordering: the first change is behavioral, the second is intellectual. If you skip the behavioral layer, the intellectual layer has nothing to attach to. A PM who has mastered frameworks but lacks gratitude, the capacity to unlearn, and genuine compassion will apply those frameworks mechanically and wonder why the results feel hollow.

## Details

### Superpower One: The Attitude of Gratitude

The first superpower is gratitude, but Shravan defines it operationally, not sentimentally. This is not gratitude as a morning journal prompt or a LinkedIn platitude. Gratitude in the cohort means: if you can help someone, you do not ask, you help. You take initiative. If a teammate has a family emergency, you pick up their work without being asked. If a fellow student is struggling with a concept, you teach them — not because the instructor told you to, but because you recognize that every act of helping someone else is also an act of strengthening the entire system.

Shravan frames this as a cultural principle that transforms the community's topology. Without gratitude, the cohort is a hub-and-spoke model — students learn from the instructor at the center, and that is it. With gratitude, the cohort becomes a mesh — students learn from every node in the network, from every conversation, from every casual observation of how a peer approaches a problem. The knowledge flow is multidirectional, and the total learning in the system far exceeds what any single instructor could deliver.

When past cohort members spontaneously congratulate incoming students — strangers messaging strangers to say "welcome, you're going to love this" — that is the ethos of gratitude in action. It is the behavior that transforms a paid program into a family, and Shravan is unapologetic about claiming that distinction: "Today, this large community of operators has become family — not like family, but truly family."

The session notes add a six-stage gratitude evolution framework that maps the journey of self-awareness with remarkable precision. **No Self** in childhood — no self-identity, shaped entirely by environment. **Some Self** — early achievements define identity ("I'm smart, I'm skilled"). **Pride** — arrogance sets in, learning stops because the person believes they already know enough. **A Reckoning** — reality provides a corrective that questions the self-worth built on pride. **Gratitude** — the realization that past success was due to others' help, to circumstances, to grace. And **No Self again at Enlightenment** — doing things for joy, without ego, because the work itself is the reward.

The accompanying quote crystallizes the entire progression: "The person who blames others is nowhere. The person who blames themselves is halfway there. The person who blames no one has arrived." This six-stage model is not just a teaching device — it is a map of the journey Shravan has taken himself, from the young PM who was proud of his IIT credential to the builder who attributes everything to "my Mahadev, whose servant I am."

### Superpower Two: The Ability to Unlearn

The second superpower is unlearning, and Shravan positions it as harder than learning — because learning adds to what you know, while unlearning requires letting go of something you have invested years in believing. Students arrive with biases accumulated across decades of professional experience. "I've been an engineering manager for 20 years, how can you teach me about APIs?" The question is sincere, and it is precisely the kind of prior that blocks new learning. The engineering manager knows APIs. What they may not know is how to think about APIs from a product perspective — which endpoints matter to users, which create lock-in, which enable the growth loops that compound.

Shravan draws a distinction that most educators miss: the difference between not speaking and active listening. Most people in the room are not speaking. Very few are actively listening — hearing the insight between the words, catching the meta-lesson beneath the surface content. Active listening requires vulnerability. It requires admitting that you might hear something that contradicts what you believed, and being willing to update your beliefs in response. This is unlearning in real time.

He frames the process as sequential: first unlearn, then learn, then keep relearning. The people who struggle most in the cohort are not those with the least experience but those with the most rigid priors — the ones who have been so successful with their existing model that they cannot imagine a better one. There is a large gap between what people think they know and what they actually know, and that gap shows up in their work.

The builder metaphor drives the point home. He tells the story of someone who critiques an architect's building design — "this space is unnecessary, that joint is ugly" — and then tries to build something themselves. Suddenly every space has a purpose and every joint exists for a structural reason. The critic did not lack intelligence. They lacked the humility that comes from actually doing the work. Unlearning is the process of replacing the critic's confidence with the builder's humility.

### Superpower Three: The Power of Compassion

The third superpower receives the most teaching time, and it is where Shravan's philosophy becomes most distinctive and most clearly connected to [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]]. He deliberately uses the word compassion — not empathy, not kindness, not generosity. Each word was considered and rejected. Empathy is feeling what someone else feels. Kindness is a disposition. Generosity is about resources. Compassion — spoken in Sanskrit as **Karuna** — is action born from recognizing suffering. It is not a feeling. It is a commitment.

He opens by asking the class to define the difference between love and compassion, then lets students debate for ten minutes. The debate is not decoration — it is pedagogical. The answers converge on three properties: compassion is universal (not directed at one person), compassion is rooted in action (not just feeling), and compassion is selfless (without expectation of return).

But then Shravan challenges the third property with a question that makes the room uncomfortable. He asks: is anything truly selfless? Even a mother's love carries some expectation. Even the most generous act produces a feeling of satisfaction in the giver. He pushes the class to confront an uncomfortable truth: pure selflessness, as people romanticize it, does not exist in the way they imagine.

His resolution is not cynical — it is systems-level, and it is where the [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]] influence becomes most visible. Compassion means you believe the world is one system, and anything you do in the interest of the system will eventually benefit you. It is not transactional in the direct sense — you do not help someone expecting them to help you back. But it is rational at the systems level — when every participant in a system acts in the system's interest, the system as a whole improves, and every participant benefits from the improvement. He uses the analogy of fitness: when you exercise, everything improves, not just one organ. The heart does not ask for payment before pumping blood. Every organ serves the whole because that is how systems sustain themselves.

He grounds the concept with a story from his own career that makes it concrete. At a previous organization, nobody wrote documentation. Meetings were chaos. Nobody had context. As a junior PM, Shravan started writing PRDs alone. For three months, nobody cared. Nobody read them. Nobody thanked him. But by the fourth month, something shifted. In every meeting, he was the only person who was contextual, structured, and prepared. His PRDs became the reference everyone relied on. And then, gradually, the entire team started writing documentation. Compassion in action — the act of serving the system without expecting recognition — created cultural change that served everyone, including the person who started it.

He then connects this to product management with the insight that makes the Compassion Funnel more than a clever diagram. Trust is the only mechanism that builds loyalty and repeat engagement. Manipulation, peer pressure, price discounts, and novelty can drive transactions, but loyalty comes only from trust. And trust is built when you go out of your way to help without calculating the return. A product that is designed with genuine compassion for the user's situation — not "empathy" as a design-thinking buzzword but real concern for real suffering — produces a qualitatively different experience than a product designed to maximize engagement metrics.

This is the [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]] influence at its deepest. Krishna's description of the ideal devotee in Chapter 12 — "a kind friend to all living entities, free from false ego" — is the Compassion Funnel's philosophical source. The funnel merely translates the spiritual principle into professional practice: compassion leads to curiosity about the problem, curiosity leads to passion for the solution, passion leads to the critical thinking required to make the solution work. The progression is natural, and it starts not with a framework but with the willingness to see someone else's suffering as your own problem to solve.

## Impact

The Three Superpowers are not mentioned once and forgotten. They are the cultural fabric of every cohort, visible in behaviors that emerge organically because the groundwork was laid in Week 1.

When students present case studies in Bollywood-style narratives with dramatic skits and character voices, that is gratitude in operation — students bringing their full creative energy to the community because the community earned it. When senior professionals with 20+ years of experience allow a 25-year-old mentor to redirect their thinking, that is unlearning in practice — the hard-won willingness to set aside seniority in the service of growth. When Unnati, a Cohort 1 student who knew nothing about product management, helped a friend write a better PRD with no expectation of reward and received a job offer because of it, that is compassion producing outcomes at the systems level — exactly as the framework predicts.

Shravan designed these superpowers to be the invisible curriculum — the thing students discover they learned only after the program ends. When Batch 6 says "you duped us into learning human values instead of product management," they are describing the moment when they realized that the Three Superpowers were not a warm-up exercise before the real content. They were the real content. The PM frameworks were the warm-up.
