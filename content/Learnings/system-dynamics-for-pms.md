> A good system is self-preparing, self-evolving, self-purpose-seeking. Appreciate complexity, then you will be able to build simple products.

## Context

System dynamics is the capstone of Shravan's MPM curriculum, arriving in Week 7 after students have absorbed problem solving, design thinking, analytics, roadmaps, and growth. He frames the entire seven-week arc retroactively as having been about system dynamics all along -- every topic was a subsystem of a larger framework for how to see and build products. This is not an accidental reveal; it reflects his genuine conviction that systems thinking is the connective tissue of great product management, and that most PM education fails because it teaches components in isolation without showing how they interact.

## Details

Shravan introduces system dynamics as "a very complex, very, very complex field" but immediately frames his session as an introduction -- an invitation to appreciate complexity rather than master it. The central teaching is that a company is a system of systems. He draws this out concretely: there is a tech system, a product system, a sales system, a DevOps system, an operations system, and a leadership system. Each has its own internal dynamics, but the interesting behavior emerges from their interactions, not from any single system in isolation.

His knowledge model is one of the more distinctive frameworks he teaches. He introduces it in the context of explaining why toxic cultures persist and why Netflix's cutthroat culture works while others collapse. Knowledge, he argues, is a function of three things: assumptions (the starting beliefs you bring), axioms or tenets (the working principles derived from those assumptions), and actions (what you do, which generates new knowledge through integration and repetition). This three-part model explains why two companies with identical strategies can produce radically different outcomes -- their assumptions and axioms are different, so the same actions produce different knowledge.

The Netflix example is telling. Netflix explicitly writes that "B players will be given a very generous severance package." Shravan does not condemn this as toxic; instead he uses it to illustrate that the ethos -- the underlying axioms -- matters more than the surface behavior. Netflix's culture works because the assumptions and axioms are aligned with the actions. Companies where the written values contradict the lived experience are the ones that become truly toxic, because the system produces contradictory signals that degrade trust.

He returns to a theme from earlier weeks but now frames it in systems language: retrospective understanding is easy, prospective understanding is what separates builders from observers. He uses an analogy about 4G and 5G -- visionary people look at things proactively, while 99% of the world looks retrospectively, and retrospective understanding is not a mark of intelligence. The system dynamics lens explains why: in a complex system, understanding an outcome after it occurs is trivial because you can trace the causal chain backward. Predicting the outcome requires understanding the system's dynamics, feedback loops, and emergent properties -- a fundamentally harder task.

The Adam 1 and Adam 2 framework, which he introduces in Week 5 Session 2 during a philosophical tangent that turns out to be deeply connected to his systems thinking, describes two types of human motivation. Adam 1 is the intellect-driven self -- the next role, the next promotion, the next achievement. Adam 2 is the values-driven self -- humility, contentment, compassion, recognition that there will always be someone better. Shravan uses this to argue that most people optimize for Adam 1 because it is tangible and measurable, while Adam 2's benefits (inner calm, resilience, trust) are not tangible but are far more durable. In systems terms, Adam 1 is a metric and Adam 2 is a system property -- you cannot optimize for it directly, but it emerges from consistent right action.

The phrase "appreciate complexity, then you will be able to build simple products" captures Shravan's core conviction about system dynamics and PM practice. Most people appreciate simplicity -- they want frameworks, checklists, templates. His counter-argument is that genuine simplicity only comes from deeply understanding the complexity beneath it. If you do not understand why a system behaves the way it does, you cannot simplify it without breaking something. This is why his curriculum spends seven weeks building foundational understanding before arriving at system dynamics: without that foundation, the concept of interacting subsystems would be abstract rather than experiential.

He connects this directly to how students should approach product analysis. When he says "if you know that lens, you can break down any product," he means the system dynamics lens: identify the components, identify the nodes, understand how they interact, look for the loops (reinforcing and degenerative), and understand which properties are emergent. He acknowledges this is "not easy to do -- it is an art," which is a rare admission of difficulty from someone who typically frames things as achievable with discipline.

## Impact

System dynamics is the intellectual throughline of everything Shravan teaches. His analytics framework is a subsystem of it (metrics are signals about system behavior). His growth loops are a subsystem of it (loops are feedback mechanisms within systems). His design thinking is a subsystem of it (design translates system intentions into user experience). By revealing this architecture in Week 7, he gives students a meta-framework that makes all the individual topics more powerful in combination than they were in isolation. The "appreciate complexity" mantra has become one of the most-quoted phrases from the cohort, because it reframes the anxiety students feel about complex problems into a productive orientation: complexity is not a bug, it is the terrain where great PMs operate.
