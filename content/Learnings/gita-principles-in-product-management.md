> How an ancient conversation on a battlefield became a product management handbook.

## Context

Shravan places the Bhagavad Gita As It Is alongside Inspired by Marty Cagan and High Output Management by Andy Grove in every reading list he has ever published. Never in a separate "spiritual" section, always mixed into the professional canon. This is not a metaphorical gesture. Specific verses from the Gita map directly onto the frameworks he teaches in his [[mpm-cohort-pedagogy]], and the connections are precise enough to warrant tracing them verse by verse.

## Details

### Nishkama Karma as Product Sense

The verse that appears most often in Shravan's teaching, whether he cites it by number or not, is Bhagavad Gita 2.47: "You have a right to perform your prescribed duty, but you are not entitled to the fruits of action. Never consider yourself the cause of the results of your activities, and never be attached to not doing your duty." Srila Prabhupada's purport on this verse distinguishes three categories of action: prescribed duties, capricious work, and inaction. In Shravan's PM vocabulary, these map cleanly onto solving real user problems (prescribed duty), building features for vanity metrics (capricious work), and analysis paralysis (inaction).

His most-repeated PM teaching, "Be passionate about the problem space, not the compensation," is 2.47 restated for a product audience. The instruction is not to be indifferent to outcomes but to anchor effort in the quality of the work itself rather than its rewards. In his [[product-management-philosophy]], this surfaces as the founder mindset: caring deeply about the user's pain, not about the press release or the promotion that might follow.

The companion verse, 2.48, extends the principle: "Perform your duty equipoised, O Arjuna, abandoning all attachment to success or failure. Such equanimity is called yoga." Prabhupada's purport explains that gain or victory are Krishna's concern; Arjuna is simply advised to act according to the dictation. For a product manager shipping features into uncertain markets, this is a remarkably practical framework. You control the discovery process, the user research rigor, the engineering quality. You do not control whether the market moves, whether the competitor ships first, or whether the board changes strategy. Equanimity across success and failure is not detachment from caring; it is the discipline of placing effort where you have jurisdiction.

### Karma Yoga and the Builder's Obligation

Chapter 3 contains the Gita's strongest argument against inaction, and it maps directly to Shravan's [[building-philosophy]] of "Do, then report." Verse 3.5 states that no one can refrain from doing something even for a moment, because everyone is forced to act by the qualities acquired from the modes of material nature. Verse 3.8 delivers the practical instruction: "Perform your prescribed duty, for doing so is better than not working. One cannot even maintain one's physical body without work."

This is the spiritual foundation beneath Shravan's antipathy toward what he calls "PM theater" -- the meetings, the alignment decks, the strategy sessions that produce no tangible output. His rule "Do, then report" is a secular restatement of Krishna's instruction to Arjuna: the soul is always active, so direct that activity toward prescribed work rather than allowing it to dissipate into speculation. In his cohort, he teaches this through the dollar-value-of-time exercise: if you earn a certain amount per hour, every hour spent in non-productive work is a quantifiable loss. The Gita's frame is deeper -- it is not about money but about the nature of the soul -- but the practical instruction converges.

Verse 3.9 adds the dimension of service: "Work done as a sacrifice for Visnu has to be performed; otherwise work causes bondage in this material world." Prabhupada's purport explains that any work not done for the satisfaction of the Supreme becomes bondage, whether the work itself appears good or evil. In Shravan's application, building products as seva (service) rather than for personal glory is what separates sustainable builders from burnout candidates. His teaching company [[rethink-systems]] operates on this principle: the work is offered as service to students, not as a vehicle for personal brand expansion.

### Verse 3.19 and the Detached Builder

The verse Shravan lives most visibly is 3.19: "Therefore, without being attached to the fruits of activities, one should act as a matter of duty, for by working without attachment one attains the Supreme." Prabhupada's purport explains that to act on behalf of the Supreme is to act without attachment for the result, and that this is perfect action of the highest degree.

The entire [[content-flywheel]] -- Build, Learn, Teach, Distribute, Build Better -- is this verse in motion. Shravan builds and teaches not because any single output guarantees a specific result, but because the cycle itself is the work. When the Builder's Bible reached 198,000 words, when PrabhupadaAI reached its first users, when Predict My Layoff went viral -- none of these outcomes were guaranteed at the start. The work was done as a matter of duty, and the results followed as they would.

### Equanimity in the Product Lifecycle

Chapter 2 contains a sequence on equanimity that maps to the emotional volatility of product work. Verse 2.14 teaches that the nonpermanent appearance of happiness and distress are like the appearance and disappearance of winter and summer seasons, and one must learn to tolerate them without being disturbed. Verse 2.56 describes the sage of steady mind as one who is not disturbed even amidst the threefold miseries, nor elated when there is happiness, free from attachment, fear, and anger.

Every product manager knows the emotional whiplash of launch weeks: the euphoria of positive metrics, the despair of a critical bug, the anxiety of competitor movement, the frustration of stakeholder misalignment. Shravan's teaching on resilience -- "How you do anything is how you do everything" -- is an operational restatement of sthita-dhir muni, the sage of steady mind. The instruction is not to suppress emotion but to remain steady in one's prescribed work regardless of whether the current season is winter or summer.

### The Qualities of a Devotee as PM Leadership Traits

Chapter 12, verses 13-14, describe the devotee who is "dear to Me" with a list of qualities: nonenvious toward all living entities, friendly, compassionate, free from false ego, equal in happiness and distress, tolerant, always satisfied, self-controlled, engaged in service with determination, with mind and intelligence fixed. Prabhupada's purport emphasizes that such a devotee never becomes his enemy's enemy, thinking instead that the antagonism arises from his own past misdeeds.

These verses read as a leadership profile. Shravan's [[leadership-views]] emphasize empathy over authority, suggestions over instructions, and giving credit generously. The specific quality of not becoming one's enemy's enemy is the spiritual root of his "Compassion Funnel" framework described in [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]]: see suffering, develop curiosity, build passion, create a solution. The funnel begins with compassion -- the same karunam (kindness) that 12.13 places as a defining trait of the devotee.

### Surrender and Svadharma

Chapter 18 brings the Gita to its conclusion with two pivotal instructions. Verse 18.45 states that by following his qualities of work, every man can become perfect. Verse 18.66 delivers the ultimate instruction: "Abandon all varieties of religion and just surrender unto Me. I shall deliver you from all sinful reactions. Do not fear."

Shravan's relationship with his own svadharma (nature-aligned duty) is visible in his career arc. He did not become a product manager through a linear credential path; he built his way into it, from a Waze-like app in Arizona to Flipkart to founding [[rethink-systems]]. Each step was an expression of his natural qualities -- building, teaching, connecting. The Gita's instruction is that perfection comes from following one's own nature, not from imitating another's path. Prabhupada's purport on 18.66 promises that simple surrender makes all other purificatory processes unnecessary. In Shravan's life, this surrender manifests as the daily chanting and 5:30 AM practice described in [[life-philosophy]] -- not as a productivity hack but as the foundation from which all work proceeds.

## Impact

The Gita is not a metaphor in Shravan's PM teaching. It is the source code. Nishkama karma explains why you should love the problem and not the promotion. Karma yoga explains why inaction is worse than imperfect action. Equanimity explains how to survive the emotional volatility of product work. The qualities of the devotee in Chapter 12 describe the leader he aspires to be. And surrender in Chapter 18 explains why the daily spiritual practice is not separate from the professional practice but its prerequisite.
