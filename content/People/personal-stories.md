## Context

Shravan Tickoo shares his life on LinkedIn with a vulnerability and warmth that is rare among professional creators with 190 million+ annual impressions. These are not thought-leadership exercises designed to generate engagement. They are memories, turning points, and moments of gratitude told in his own voice — and they form the spine of who he is and why he teaches the way he does. Across 319 posts tagged with personal stories, memories, and reflections, a clear pattern emerges: every lesson he teaches at [[rethink-systems]] traces back to a moment he lived. The Korean lady taught him compassion. The BlackBuck mentor taught him how to lead with trust. The health transformation taught him discipline. The Bhagavad Gita taught him purpose. These stories are not decorative — they are the curriculum.

## Details

### The Boy From Jammu

The story begins in **Jammu**, a tier-2 city in the foothills of the Himalayas, far from any startup map. Shravan was the kind of child who made adults simultaneously fascinated and exhausted. In 6th grade, visiting his Mamaji in Udhampur, an old tenant lady gave him the nickname "Ravan" because she could not pronounce "Shravan" — and because, she observed, "this boy breaks everything and is so curious he uproots the plants." His great-grandmother joked that he had "come to kill me" because he never, for a single moment, stopped asking questions.

By 8th grade, when the family acquired their first computer, young Shravan disappeared into a world that had nothing to do with his school curriculum — Egyptian mythology, the philosophy of music, world religions, and the elegant architecture of mathematics. In 10th grade, while his classmates focused single-mindedly on board exam preparation, he was reading about Hilbert's problems and the design of mathematical systems. His relatives stopped inviting him to their homes, concerned that his unconventional curiosity would be a disruptive influence on their own children.

What makes this story foundational is not the curiosity itself — many children are curious — but what it became. The boy who was "too curious" for his family grew into the teacher who tells 10,000+ students that curiosity is the single quality no one can fake and no credential can replace. The Jammu thread runs through every milestone in his career. When Favikon ranked him the #1 PM creator in India, his response was not a celebratory post but a reflection: "Coming from a tier-2 city like Jammu, I never thought my voice would resonate with so many people." The boy from Jammu is always the protagonist of the story, no matter how large the stage becomes.

### The Arizona App — First Taste of Building

Before Flipkart, before the PM career, before the YouTube channel, Shravan built a **location-tracking app** similar to Waze. It was approximately 2014-2015. The app went viral in Arizona, United States — without a single dollar of marketing spend, without a launch strategy, without a press mention. It simply worked, and people found it.

This was the first time Shravan experienced the magic of building something useful and watching an audience materialize around it. The fact that it happened in a foreign market, organically, with zero promotion, planted a seed that would grow into the central thesis of his career: build something genuinely useful, and the audience finds you. This is the earliest evidence of what he would later formalize as the [[content-flywheel]] — value creates its own distribution. Every cohort hears this story, because it proves the principle with no asterisks: no connections, no money, no marketing team — just a product that solved a real problem.

### Korea: The Stranger Who Became Family (2014)

During a research internship at **KAIST** in Daejeon, South Korea, Shravan fell seriously ill. He had no working phone — his Indian SIM was useless. He had no accessible cash — his ATM card was not chip-based, and Indian currency could not be exchanged at local banks. He had no one who spoke English nearby. Alone, unwell, and unable to communicate, he sat in a park trying to figure out his next move.

A Korean lady saw him. Without sharing a single word of common language, she understood the situation. She held his hand, communicated through gestures and patience, walked him to a medicine shop, bought him medicine and food out of her own pocket, brought him back to his room, and stayed with him until he was well — "as like my own mother," he writes.

He still calls her every three to four months. She treats him like a son. They have never needed a shared language to maintain a relationship that has lasted over a decade.

This story is the emotional bedrock of everything Shravan teaches about compassion and service. When he tells cohort students that "there is wealth you earn which is far deeper than money, which is trust and love and you earn that for the rest of your life," he is not reciting a motivational quote — he is describing a Korean woman in a park who showed him what unconditional kindness looks like. The story directly feeds [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]], because the Korean lady demonstrated the Vaishnava principle of seva (selfless service) years before Shravan had a word for it. And it feeds [[three-superpowers]], because the "Power of Compassion" that he teaches as the third superpower is not abstract philosophy — it is what he experienced in a park in Daejeon when a stranger chose to care.

### The BlackBuck Mentor and the Three Golden Rules

At **BlackBuck**, in what was his first dedicated product management role, Shravan was a junior PM pouring everything he had into proving himself. He worked late nights, coordinated obsessively, managed every detail personally. He was determined to succeed. Then a senior PM from a different product team — someone with no obligation to help — noticed the intensity and pulled Shravan aside over lunch. "Shravan, tere pass 5 min hai baat karne ke liye."

What followed was a five-minute conversation that reshaped Shravan's entire approach to leadership. Three lessons, delivered simply and directly. First: **compassion over control** — "When you are compassionate, you look outward towards the people who work with you and make them happy — and rest assured they will do everything by design to keep you happy." Second: **autonomy breeds excellence** — "Developers and designers will be your winners when you allow them autonomy of work, give them space, and trust their judgment." Third: **control what you can** — "What's not in your hand should not be in your head. Focus on the variables which you can change and be awesome at that — that's all that matters."

Shravan went on to become one of the highest-performing junior PMs at BlackBuck, earning Employee of the Year. He has retold this story publicly at least four times across 2021-2023, always with the same gratitude: "He may not remember me, but he was a mentor to me." The story is the origin point of the [[leadership-views]] he now teaches — every lesson about empathy, autonomy, and focus traces back to a lunchtime conversation with a colleague who chose to invest five minutes in a younger PM.

### The 100% Hike Story (2019)

In 2019, Shravan interviewed for a Senior PM role at a startup and did not perform well in the initial round. The structure was not there. He was nervous. But instead of accepting the result, he asked for one more day.

He spent the next **15 hours** doing deep primary and secondary research — the company's website, business model, competitive landscape, user journeys, market positioning. He wrote a comprehensive PRD that demonstrated not just his understanding of the product but his vision for where it could go. The founder called back within two hours.

The founder's words, in Hindi, became one of Shravan's most-quoted lines: "Lakde, maine zindagi main ek cheez seekhi hai — jo log apne passion ke liye sab kuch peeche chod dete hai, woh log zindagi main zaroor kuch acha karte hai." ("Boy, I've learned one thing in life — people who leave everything behind for their passion always end up doing something great.") The founder offered him a 50% hike on top of his already-expected salary. "A founder gets paid like a founder."

This story is about initiative and preparation — the willingness to invest 15 hours of work into a second chance. He tells it to every cohort as evidence that product sense is not innate talent but demonstrated commitment: "You are not the founder, but definitely should be founder material."

### The Health Transformation (2025)

In April 2025, Shravan's doctor delivered a sentence that changed the trajectory of his year: "If you continue like this, your body will die before your startup does." [[rethink-systems]] was growing faster than ever — 14 to 16 hour days of teaching, traveling, content creation. His weight had crossed 105 kg. He fainted. A full health check revealed that every metric needed attention.

What followed was one of the most disciplined personal transformations in his story — and it happened in public, on LinkedIn, where his audience watched it unfold in real time. Starting June 2025, he walked 7,000 steps daily. Then 10,000. Then 20,000. Then **30,000 steps a day for sixty consecutive days**. He shifted to OMAD (one meal a day), protein-heavy. He started waking at 5:30 AM, chanting, sitting quietly — the spiritual practice and the physical discipline merging into a single morning architecture.

Three months later: 82 kg, approximately 16% body fat, clean reports across every metric. "The real transformation wasn't physical," he writes. "I could run again. Play again. Be present in my own life. At 32, I felt alive — properly alive."

The health transformation is inseparable from the spiritual deepening documented in [[the-spiritual-foundation]]. The 5:30 AM waking time became chanting time. The walks became meditation. The discipline became devotion. "It wasn't motivation that saved me," he reflects. "It was humility. Faith. And the refusal to quit." The Bhagavad Gita's prescription for regulated habits — what you eat, how you sleep, the rhythm of your days (BG 6.16-17) — became a lived practice, not a philosophical reference.

### The Stray Dog and the Compassion Funnel

One of the most revealing stories — and the one that most directly bridges his personal life and his professional framework — is the stray dog story from 2021. Seeing an injured animal on the street triggered compassion. That compassion led to curiosity about the problem space — how many stray animals are there? What happens to them? What systems exist to help? That curiosity led to passion for solving the problem at scale. That passion led to the concept of building a curated pet adoption marketplace. And that sequence — compassion to curiosity to passion to critical thinking to building — became the **Compassion Funnel**, one of his signature teaching frameworks.

The framework is not an intellectual exercise. It is the emotional autobiography of how Shravan approaches every problem he has ever worked on. The Korean lady story is the compassion origin. The Arizona app (curiosity about location-tracking becoming a product) follows the same funnel. [[prabhupada-ai]] (devotion becoming an AI product) follows the identical pattern. "You don't need a lot of frameworks to become a better PM," he teaches in every cohort. "You just have to be a better human to become a better PM." The Compassion Funnel is his bridge between [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]] and his professional life — the place where the Gita's emphasis on selfless action meets the PM's craft of building for others.

### The Bus Stop Father

On a bus from Chandigarh to Jammu, Shravan overheard a Punjabi uncle telling his son on the phone: "Koi ni puttar, jo honda hai waheguru ke karam se hi honda hai." The uncle's son had just gone through a difficult job search. Shravan introduced himself, shared his YouTube channel, and moved on without thinking much of it.

Days later, the son called. He had been watching the channel, grown in confidence, and landed his first product role. But the real story was not the son's success — it was his father's transformation. "My father used to worry a lot about me," the son told Shravan. "In the last few weeks, he has been keeping fit and laughing, because I told him I am feeling confident day by day. Somehow my confidence grew his confidence."

This is the story Shravan tells when he wants to illustrate the second-order effects of teaching. A YouTube video does not just reach the viewer. It reaches the viewer's family. The confidence it builds in one person radiates outward — to parents, partners, children. The bus stop father story is why Shravan keeps creating content even when the metrics do not spike, because the impact is often invisible and delayed, rippling through families and lives he will never meet.

### The Bhagavad Gita Turning Point (2020)

If the personal stories have a single hinge point, it is 2020. Shravan was at a crossroads in his professional life — successful on paper, but searching for something deeper. He had four job changes behind him and a well-compensated PM role, but the inner narrative had not caught up with the external success. He was asking himself whether credentials and titles were the point, or whether there was something more fundamental.

Then he read the **Bhagavad Gita As It Is** by A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada. "In which Lord Krsna spoke that compassion was the key to happiness, when you help others without expectation — bliss manifests." The effect was transformative. He stopped measuring himself by institutional markers and started focusing entirely on service — how he could help the next person become better. "I didn't care now if I was from IIT R or B. The only thing I cared about was how I can help the next person better."

This turning point explains why [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]] runs through everything Shravan builds. The Gita did not just inform his philosophy — it reoriented his entire sense of purpose. The health transformation of 2025, five years later, produced the physical version of the same reorientation: daily chanting, God-consciousness, waking at 5:30 AM. But the spiritual turning point of 2020 came first, and everything that followed — the teaching mission, the devotional AI product, the warmth in his public voice — flows from that single reading experience.

### The Gaming Origin Story

He grew up playing PSP, Nintendo 64, and PS1 — but never the action games his friends preferred. He was drawn to simulation games: **Age of Empires**, **SimCity**, and titles that "rewarded strategy over speed." Hours spent managing virtual civilizations, allocating resources, responding to crises, and optimizing systems taught him something no textbook could: "The biggest difference between simulation games and most other genres is fundamental — there is no end, there is no finish point. Playing the game, not winning it, is the point."

He credits these games with developing the PM instincts he would later teach: reading complex systems, making resource allocation decisions under uncertainty, thinking three moves ahead while responding to the crisis in front of you. The parallel between a well-designed simulation game and a well-designed product — both are systems where the user's experience emerges from invisible rules — became a teaching reference he still uses in cohort sessions.

### The NSS Roots of Teaching

The earliest evidence of Shravan's teaching instinct predates his PM career by a full decade. At IIT Roorkee from 2011 to 2015, he volunteered with the **National Service Scheme (NSS)**, teaching mathematics to underprivileged students. This was not a resume-building exercise or a college club commitment. At ages 18 to 22, before he had any professional identity, before the YouTube channel or the LinkedIn following or the cohort programs, the impulse to teach was already the constant.

The NSS experience is the proof that [[teaching-and-mentoring]] is intrinsic to who Shravan is — not a growth strategy he adopted when content creation became profitable. When he later capped his MPM cohort at 25 students per group and insisted on a gurukul-inspired scaling model through trained mentors rather than larger classrooms, the sensibility traces back to teaching math to small groups of students who needed someone to show up for them — not at scale, not for an audience, but face-to-face, with patience.

### The Founding of Rethink Systems (2024)

After nine years as a product operator across Flipkart, BlackBuck, Magicbricks, Edureka, BYJU'S, and Bhanzu, Shravan made the leap to build his own company. But [[rethink-systems]] did not start as a company — it started as a WhatsApp group called "Art & Product Musings" that grew to 3,000+ active members with 100+ writing daily. The community came first. The product came from the community.

He names his early bosses and mentors who made the trajectory possible: **Ravi Karthik** taught him empathy and patience. **Indraneel Bommisetty** taught him creativity. **Lovleen Bhatia** taught him how to face adversity. The gratitude is always specific and always public. And the sign-off is always the same: "All my love and gratitude go to my wonderful students — and to my Mahadev, whose servant I am."

The company was registered as Naum Infinitium Private Limited in February 2024, headquartered in Bengaluru. "Profitable from day 1" — not as a brag, but as a founding principle. The first cohort produced over $150,000 in revenue with zero customer acquisition cost, 100% from organic community. By April 2026: $1M+ revenue, 10,000+ PMs mentored, 150+ career transitions, 400+ operator community, and Cohort 8 launching September 2026.

The BlackBuck mentor taught him how to lead with trust. The health transformation taught him discipline. The Bhagavad Gita taught him purpose.
