> "You find the treasures of life when you don't look for them and start believing the pursuit is the treasure."

## Context

Shravan shares his life on LinkedIn with a vulnerability that is rare for a professional creator with 300M+ impressions. These are not thought-leadership exercises — they are confessions, memories, and turning points told in his own voice. From a boy in Jammu who lied about his IIT branch, to a founder who fainted from neglecting his body, to a traveler saved by a Korean stranger's kindness — these stories form the spine of who he is and why he teaches the way he does.

## Details

### The Boy From Jammu

Shravan grew up in **Jammu**, a tier-2 city in northern India, and describes himself as relentlessly curious from childhood. In 6th grade, visiting his Mamaji in Udhampur, an old tenant lady called him "Ravan" because she could not say "Shravan." She used to say this boy breaks everything and is so curious he uproots the plants. His great-grandmother joked he had "come to kill me" because of his relentless pestering with questions.

By 8th grade, when the family got their first computer, he was reading about "Egyptian mythology, music, religion, mathematics — basically anything besides my academics." In 10th grade, while preparing for boards, he was simultaneously reading books on the design of mathematics and Hilbert's problems. Relatives avoided inviting him to their homes, worried he would be a bad influence on their children. This pattern — deep curiosity, unconventional interests, going against what society expected — is the origin story he returns to again and again.

### The Arizona App — First Entrepreneurial Venture

Before Flipkart, before the PM career, Shravan built a **location-tracking app** similar to Waze. It went viral in Arizona without any marketing spend. This was his first taste of building something that people actually used at scale. The fact that it spread organically, in a foreign market, with zero promotion, validated the instinct that would later define his career: build something genuinely useful, and the audience finds you. This is the earliest evidence of the [[content-flywheel]] principle — value creates its own distribution. The app predates his formal entry into product management, making it a formative entrepreneurial experience that shaped his builder identity.

### IIT Roorkee and the Imposter Syndrome

He joined **IIT Roorkee** for Metallurgy — not IIT Bombay, not IIT Delhi, not Computer Science. This ate at him for years. "I used to disgust with the fact that I was studying at IIT Roorkee instead of IIT B or D and had my discipline as 'Metallurgy' and not 'CS'."

He began lying about it. His rehearsed answers became "IIT Bombay, CS" or "IIT Delhi, Mech" or "Google, Microsoft." The imposter syndrome followed him through four job changes and a well-compensated PM role that still left him depressed and lonely. The breakthrough came when he read the **Bhagavad Gita** in 2020 and started helping others: "I didn't care now if I was from IIT R or B. The only thing I cared about was how I can help the next person better."

### Korea: The Stranger Who Became Family (2014)

During a research internship at **KAIST** in Daejeon, South Korea, Shravan fell seriously ill. He had no working phone, no cash — his ATM card was not chip-based, and Indian currency could not be exchanged — and no one who spoke English nearby. "Completely broken, I sat in a park crying in pain and agony."

A Korean lady saw him, held his hand, communicated through signals, took him to a medicine shop, bought him medicine and food, brought him back to his room, and stayed until he was fine — "as like my own mother." He still calls her every three to four months. She treats him like a son.

This story is the emotional foundation for his teaching philosophy: "There is wealth you earn which is far deeper than money, which is trust and love and you earn that for the rest of your life."

### The Blackbuck Mentor and the Three Golden Rules

At his first PM role at **Blackbuck**, Shravan was a junior PM trying desperately to prove himself. He worked late nights, micromanaged engineers, pushed too hard, and did not realize he was becoming "the dictator PM." Developers started ghosting him. His release failed. Then a senior PM from a different product team noticed his distress and pulled him aside at lunch: "Shravan, tere pass 5 min hai baat karne ke liye."

Three lessons came from that conversation that shaped everything after. First, **compassion over control**: "When you are compassionate, you look outward towards the people who work with you and make them happy — and rest assured they will do everything by design to keep you happy." Second, **autonomy breeds excellence**: "Developers and designers will be your winners when you allow them autonomy of work, give them space, and trust their judgment." Third, **control what you can**: "What's not in your hand should not be in your head."

He went on to become one of the best-performing junior PMs at Blackbuck. He has retold this story at least four times across years (2021-2023), always with the same gratitude: "He may not remember me, but he was a mentor to me."

### The 100% Hike Story (2019)

He bombed an interview with a startup founder for a Senior PM role. "I miserably failed, I couldn't structure the answer." He was shivering. But he asked for one more day.

He spent 15 hours doing deep primary and secondary research — website, business model, competitors, user journeys — and wrote a comprehensive PRD. The founder called back within two hours. The founder's words, in Hindi: "Lakde, maine zindagi main ek cheez seekhi hai — jo log apne passion ke liye sab kuch peeche chod dete hai, woh log zindagi main zaroor kuch acha karte hai." The founder gave him a 50% hike on top of his expected salary. "A founder gets paid like a founder." This story is about initiative and persistence, and he retells it as a lesson to aspiring PMs: "You are not the founder but definitely should be founder material."

### The Health Crisis (2025)

In April 2025, Shravan's doctor told him: "If you continue like this, your body will die before your startup does."

[[rethink-systems]] was growing fast. He was doing 14-16 hour days — teaching, traveling, creating content. His weight crossed 105 kg. He fainted. A full health check revealed everything wrong: cholesterol, triglycerides, "red numbers everywhere." "That sentence broke something inside me — denial."

Starting June 2025, he walked 7,000 steps daily. Then 10K. Then 20K. Then 30K steps a day for sixty days straight. He shifted to **OMAD** (one meal a day), protein-heavy. He started waking at 5:30 AM, chanting, sitting quietly. Three months later: 82 kg, approximately 16% body fat, clean reports. "The real transformation wasn't physical. I could run again. Play again. Be present in my own life. At 32, I felt alive — properly alive."

His takeaway: "You cannot be a 1% operator at work if you're careless with life. How you do anything is how you do everything."

### The Gaming Origin Story

He grew up playing PSP, Nintendo 64, and PS1 — but not action games. He was drawn to simulation games: **Age of Empires** and titles that "rewarded strategy over speed." He credits this with developing his PM instincts: strategic thinking, resource allocation, crisis handling. "The biggest difference between simulation games and most other genres is fundamental — there is no end, there is no finish point. Playing the game, not winning it, is the point."

### PM and Mental Health

He writes openly about the mental health toll of being a PM: overwhelming ambiguity, thriving imposter syndrome, the loneliness of saying "no" to everyone. "You are a generalist wolf in a pack full of specialists." He reposted this reflection three times across 2023, each time unchanged — because the truth of it did not change.

### The Bus Stop Father

On a bus from Chandigarh to Jammu, Shravan overheard a Punjabi uncle telling his son on the phone: "Koi ni puttar, jo honda hai waheguru ke karam se hi honda hai." The uncle's son had failed a startup interview and was depressed. Shravan introduced himself, shared his YouTube channel, and forgot about it.

Days later, the son called. He had been watching the channel, grew in confidence, and cracked his first product role. But the real story was the father: "My father used to worry a lot about me. In the last few weeks, he has been keeping fit and laughing, because I told him I am feeling confident day by day. Somehow my confidence grew his confidence."

### The Founding of Rethink Systems (2024)

After 9 years as a product operator at Flipkart, Blackbuck, Times Internet, and Edureka, Shravan launched [[rethink-systems]]. What started as a WhatsApp group called "Art & Product Musings" became a cohort that produced 150+ career transitions. He names his early bosses and mentors: **Ravi Karthik** (empathy and patience), **Indraneel Bommisetty** (creativity), **Lovleen Bhatia** (facing adversity). "Profitable from day 1." His gratitude always goes last to "my Mahadev, whose servant I am."

### Ranked #1 PM Creator in India (2024)

Favikon ranked him the best Product Management creator in India and 3rd worldwide on LinkedIn. He frames it not as personal triumph but community proof: "Coming from a tier-2 city like Jammu, I never thought my voice would resonate with so many people." He lists the milestones — speaking at IITs, IIMs, ISB, NUS; 500+ student transitions; investing in 20+ startups across 7+ countries; 300M+ LinkedIn impressions. "If a boy from Jammu can do this, we can achieve anything."

### The Young India Fellow

Between IIT Roorkee and his corporate career, Shravan was selected as a Young India Fellow through the Ashoka University and University of Pennsylvania collaboration -- a program with a less than 2% acceptance rate. This fellowship, designed for multidisciplinary thinkers, reinforced the same pattern visible from his childhood: the generalist curiosity that made relatives worry about him as a kid became, in the right institutional context, a competitive advantage. The fellowship exposed him to cross-disciplinary thinking -- the same kind of thinking that would later make his PM teaching distinctive, drawing from the Bhagavad Gita, simulation games, mathematics, and Korean kindness in the same breath. He does not reference the fellowship often on LinkedIn, preferring to lead with his work rather than his credentials, but it represents a formative period where his intellectual range was recognized and validated for the first time in a formal setting.

### The Stray Dog and the Compassion Funnel

One of the most revealing personal stories -- and the one that most directly connects his emotional life to his professional framework -- is the stray dog story from 2021. Seeing an injured animal on the street triggered compassion, which led to curiosity about the problem space (how many strays are there? what happens to them?), which led to passion for scaling a solution, which led to the concept of building a curated pet adoption marketplace. He named the progression the Compassion Funnel and framed it as the real source of product sense: compassion leads to curiosity, curiosity leads to passion, passion leads to critical thinking, critical thinking leads to building.

The framework is not abstract. It is the emotional autobiography of how Shravan approaches every problem. The Korean lady story is the compassion origin. The Arizona app (curiosity about location-tracking becoming a product) is the same funnel. [[prabhupada-ai]] (devotion becoming an AI product) follows the identical pattern. "You don't need a lot of frameworks to become a better PM," he teaches. "You just have to be a better human to become a better PM." The Compassion Funnel is his bridge between [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]] and his professional life -- the place where the Bhagavad Gita's emphasis on detachment from outcomes meets the PM's job of building for others.

### The Favikon Ranking and the Boy From Jammu

In 2024, Favikon ranked Shravan the best Product Management creator in India and third worldwide on LinkedIn. By that point, his numbers were staggering: 300M+ cumulative LinkedIn impressions, 160K+ followers, lectures at IITs, IIMs, ISB, NUS, NTU Singapore, speaking at 10+ product conferences, angel investments in 20+ startups across five countries. But his framing of this achievement is revealing. He did not post a celebratory graphic or a thought-leadership reflection on personal branding. Instead, he wrote: "Coming from a tier-2 city like Jammu, I never thought my voice would resonate with so many people."

The Jammu thread runs through every major milestone. The boy who was too curious for his relatives, who lied about his IIT branch, who sat crying in a Korean park, who fainted from neglecting his health -- that boy is always the protagonist of the story, even when the story is about being ranked third in the world. "If a boy from Jammu can do this, we can achieve anything." This is not humility as performance. It is humility as operating system -- the same humility he installs in cohort students through [[three-superpowers]], the same humility he credits with saving his life during the health crisis. The IIT Roorkee imposter syndrome never fully resolved. It transformed into something more useful: a permanent resistance to believing his own press.

### The NSS Roots of Teaching

The earliest evidence of Shravan's teaching instinct predates his PM career by a decade. At IIT Roorkee from 2011 to 2015, he volunteered with the National Service Scheme (NSS), teaching mathematics to underprivileged students. This was not a resume-building exercise or a college club commitment. At ages 18 to 22, before he had any professional identity, before the YouTube channel or the LinkedIn following or the cohort programs, the impulse to teach was already the constant. The NSS experience is the proof that [[teaching-and-mentoring]] is intrinsic to who Shravan is, not a growth strategy he adopted when content creation became profitable. When he later capped his MPM cohort at 25 students per group and insisted on a gurukul-inspired scaling model, the sensibility traces back to teaching math to small groups of students who needed someone to show up for them -- not at scale, not for an audience, but face-to-face, with patience.

### The Bhagavad Gita Turning Point (2020)

If the personal stories have a single hinge point, it is 2020. Shravan was in the grip of depression and imposter syndrome that had followed him through four job changes and a well-compensated PM role. He was lonely. He was lying about his college and his branch. His LinkedIn following was growing but the internal narrative was corrosive.

Then he read the Bhagavad Gita As It Is by A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada. "In which Lord Krsna spoke that compassion was the key to happiness, when you help others without expectation -- bliss manifests." The effect was not gradual. He stopped caring about whether he was from IIT Roorkee or IIT Bombay. He started helping others -- first through free PM content, then through the WhatsApp community, then through the cohort programs. "I didn't care now if I was from IIT R or B. The only thing I cared about was how I can help the next person better."

This turning point explains why [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]] is not a side interest but a foundational lens. The Gita did not just inform his philosophy -- it broke a psychological cycle that nothing else had been able to break. The health crisis of 2025, five years later, produced the physical version of the same transformation: daily chanting, God-consciousness, waking at 5:30 AM. But the spiritual transformation of 2020 came first, and everything that followed -- the teaching mission, the devotional AI product, the vulnerability in public posts -- flows from that single reading experience.

## Impact

These stories are not just content -- they are the curriculum. Every lesson Shravan teaches at Rethink Systems traces back to a personal failure, a moment of kindness, or a hard-won realization. The Korean lady taught him compassion. The Blackbuck mentor taught him autonomy. The health crisis taught him discipline. The imposter syndrome taught him authenticity. The Bhagavad Gita taught him purpose. The stray dog taught him that compassion is the origin of product sense. He teaches product management, but the real subject is how to live: "It took me 29 years to somehow figure out what truly made me happy."

The personal stories also function as the emotional glue of the [[rethink-systems]] community. When Batch 6 students said "you have duped us into learning human values instead of product management," they were responding not to frameworks or case studies but to the cumulative weight of these stories told over eight weeks of five-hour sessions. The vulnerability is the teaching method. The stories are the proof that the principles work, because the person teaching them has lived them -- not as curated content, but as the messy, painful, occasionally humiliating reality of becoming who you are.
