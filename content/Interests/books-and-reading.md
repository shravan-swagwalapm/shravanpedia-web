> "I have an unreasonable number of books. Happy to trade recommendations." -- LinkedIn bio

## Context

Books are not a hobby for Shravan — they are infrastructure. The way some builders rely on design systems or API libraries, Shravan relies on books as the raw material from which his thinking, his teaching, and his product instincts are constructed. He grew up as a voracious, undirected reader — Egyptian mythology, Hilbert's problems, Urdu mushairas — long before he found product management. His LinkedIn bio leads with books, not credentials. He curates and re-shares his reading lists obsessively, sometimes reposting the same book list across years with small updates, because he genuinely believes the right book at the right time changes careers.

His relationship with reading is deeply personal and traces to the earliest years. As a kid in Jammu, while everyone expected him to study for board exams, he was reading about the design of mathematics and getting lost in problems that had nothing to do with his curriculum. In college at IIT Roorkee, he went to Urdu mushairas in Delhi instead of focusing on metallurgy. He calls himself "a generalist who loved doing whatever interested me" — and reading was the engine of that generalism, the force that pulled his attention across so many domains that when he eventually encountered product management, it felt not like a new discipline but like the applied synthesis of everything he had already been reading about.

## Details

### The Sacred Text

**The Bhagavad Gita As It Is** by A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada sits at the top of every list, always. Not in a "spirituality" section — right alongside product management books. He credits it with transforming his life: "In 2020, I read the BG, in which Lord Krsna spoke that compassion was the key to happiness, when you help others without expectation — bliss manifests."

The Gita was the book that changed the direction of his life. It reframed his understanding of success, purpose, and duty in a way that no PM book had ever approached. The concept of nishkama karma — action without attachment to results — became his operational philosophy for building and teaching. The concept of dharma — prescribed duty — became his framework for deciding what to build and what to ignore. The concept of karuna — compassion — became the Compassion Funnel he teaches in every cohort. It is not just a recommendation; it is the foundation of his entire life philosophy. See [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]] for the full treatment of how this text shapes his work.

### The PM Canon

Shravan has published detailed PM book lists repeatedly since 2022, and the titles that appear most consistently form a clear canon — the books he would save if the library caught fire.

In **product thinking**, he returns again and again to *Creativity, Inc* by Ed Catmull ("putting 10x into practice"), *Made to Stick* by Chip and Dan Heath, *The Lean Product Playbook* by Dan Olsen ("one of the best books for learning product management for aspirants"), *In The Plex* by Steven Levy, and *Inspired* by Marty Cagan. Each of these books taught him something specific: Catmull taught him that creativity requires organizational courage, not just individual talent. Heath taught him that ideas that stick follow identifiable patterns. Olsen gave him the structured approach to validation that his early career lacked. Levy showed him how a company's culture and its products are inseparable.

For **product practice**, the essentials are *Continuous Discovery Habits* by Teresa Torres (the book that changed how he thinks about research), *Getting To Yes* ("the best book on negotiation I have seen"), *Sprint* by Jake Knapp, and *Getting Things Done* by David Allen. In **product leadership**, *High Output Management* by Andy Grove ("still a classic"), *Empowered* by Marty Cagan, and *Product Leadership* by Banfield and Eriksson anchor the shelf.

His **design thinking** recommendations center on *Don't Make Me Think* by Steve Krug, *The Design of Everyday Things* by Don Norman ("still a classic, will always be a classic"), and *The Design Thinking Playbook* by Michael Lewrick. For **culture and people**, he recommends *Radical Candor* by Kim Scott, *No Rules Rules* by Reed Hastings, and *The Captain Class* by Sam Walker. And then there are the wildcards — non-PM books he insists PMs must read: *On Writing* by Stephen King ("PMs need to be great writers"), *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* by Ben Horowitz, *Team of Rivals* ("one of the best books written on how to influence without authority"), *Start With Why* by Simon Sinek, and *Chatter* by Ethan Kross.

### The Extended Library

In a May 2022 post, he published a much deeper list organized by discipline — Analytics, Business, Design, Engineering, Product Management — recommending 30+ books including *Blue Ocean Strategy*, *Algorithms to Live By*, *100 Things Every Designer Needs to Know About People*, *Behave* by Robert Sapolsky, *Chaos: Making a New Science* by James Gleick, *The Mythical Man-Month* ("old yet relevant"), *Escaping the Build Trap* by Melissa Perri, and *Business Model Generation* by Alexander Osterwalder. Honorable mentions that he references but does not always include in the essential lists are *Hooked* by Nir Eyal, *Crossing the Chasm*, and *Built to Last*.

### Reading as Identity

His childhood reading pattern reveals something important about who he became: he was never a disciplined reader who worked through assigned lists. He read what fascinated him — mythology, mathematics, music, religion — and the breadth became his superpower. "I was always a generalist who loved doing whatever interested me." The kid who read about Egyptian gods and Hilbert's problems and attended Urdu mushairas was building a neural network of cross-domain connections that would eventually make him the kind of PM who teaches with examples from Age of Empires, the Bhagavad Gita, Korean kindness, and startup salary negotiations in the same breath.

Reading across domains is what eventually made him a PM who can connect ideas that other PMs cannot see. The Compassion Funnel draws on spiritual philosophy. The [[four-layers-of-design]] framework draws on cognitive psychology. The teaching style draws on storytelling traditions. The product thinking draws on systems theory. None of these connections would exist in a reader who stayed within the PM section of the bookstore.

### Articles He Recommends

Beyond books, Shravan curates essential article lists that he reshares regularly: "In Defense of Eye Candy" (design), "TikTok and the Sorting Hat" (product), "The Psychology of Design" from A List Apart, "How Superhuman Built an Engine to Find Product/Market Fit", and "What Buddhism Taught Me About Product Management" by Lenny Rachitsky. Each article represents a specific insight that books alone do not capture — the specificity of a case study, the recency of a contemporary product analysis, or the cross-pollination of ideas from adjacent disciplines.

## The Library (435 Books — Apple Books)

In April 2026, a full inventory of Shravan's Apple Books library confirmed the "unreasonable number" claim: **435 books**. The complete categorized collection reveals far more about who he is than any LinkedIn bio could. It is a map of a mind.

### Spiritual / Philosophy / Eastern Thought (35+ books)

The largest category by emotional weight, spanning Hindu, Buddhist, Sufi, Stoic, and mystical traditions — not as academic study but as lived practice. The foundational text is *Bhagavad-gita As It Is* by Prabhupada (see [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]]). The collection extends through *Bhagavad Gita In the Light of Kashmir Shaivism*, *The Journey Home* by Radhanath Swami, *Autobiography of a Yogi* by Paramahansa Yogananda, multiple Buddhist texts (*In the Buddha's Words*, *What the Buddha Taught*, *Old Path White Clouds*), Kashmir Shaivism (*The Secret Supreme*), Sadhguru (*Inner Engineering*, *A Taste of Well-Being*), contemplative works (*The Power of Now*, *The Untethered Soul*, *The Surrender Experiment*), Sufi poetry (*The Poetry of Kabir*, *Mirabai: Ecstatic Poems*), and Japanese warrior philosophy (*Hagakure*, *The Book of Five Rings*).

The breadth is significant. This is not a person reading within a single tradition. It is a person searching for wisdom across every tradition that has produced it, while remaining rooted in one. The Hindu texts dominate, but the Buddhist, Sufi, Celtic, Japanese, and Stoic branches reach outward from that center, creating a spiritual library that is both grounded and expansive.

### Stoicism / Western Philosophy (20 books)

A full Stoic library — not just the popular titles but primary sources. Two copies of *Meditations* by Marcus Aurelius sit alongside *Letters From a Stoic*, *Enchiridion*, and Ryan Holiday's complete shelf (*The Daily Stoic*, *Ego Is the Enemy*, *The Obstacle Is the Way*, *Stillness Is the Key*, *Discipline Is Destiny*, *Lives of the Stoics*). The collection also includes *The Consolation of Philosophy*, *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*, *The Courage to Be Disliked*, and *Xenophon's Cyrus the Great*.

The Stoic shelf connects directly to the Gita in Shravan's reading — both traditions teach equanimity, discipline, and detachment from outcomes. Marcus Aurelius's morning reflection practice parallels Shravan's 5:30 AM chanting. The Stoic emphasis on controlling what is within your power and accepting what is not maps to the Gita's nishkama karma. Two ancient traditions, separated by thousands of miles and centuries, arriving at the same operational wisdom.

### Product Management / Business (35+ books)

The professional toolkit, with every major PM framework represented: *High Output Management*, *Radical Candor*, *Inspired*, *Zero to One*, *The Lean Startup*, *Working Backwards*, *The Hard Thing About Hard Things*, *Creativity Inc*, *No Rules Rules*, *Blitzscaling*, and *Good to Great*. The AI-specific shelf includes *The AI Product Manager's Handbook* and *The AI Product Playbook*. Interview prep is covered by *Cracking the PM Interview* and *Decode and Conquer*. Growth and metrics are represented by *Radical Focus*, *Hacking Growth*, and *Traction*.

### Science / Math / Physics (30+ books)

This is where the library becomes unusual for a PM. Fifteen books on prime numbers, infinity, chaos theory, and mathematical biography. *Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman* appears twice — a hero. The shelf includes *The Man Who Knew Infinity* (Ramanujan), *Fermat's Last Theorem*, *Perfect Rigor* (two copies), *Prime Obsession*, *The Music of the Primes*, *God Created The Integers*, *A Mathematician's Apology*, *Is God a Mathematician?*, and *Chaos: Making a New Science*.

For a PM who studied metallurgy, this collection is deeply unusual and points back to the kid in Jammu who was reading about Hilbert's problems instead of studying for boards. The mathematics shelf is not professional development. It is the truest expression of who Shravan is when nobody is watching — a person who finds beauty in prime numbers, who reads about Ramanujan for pleasure, who keeps two copies of *Perfect Rigor* because one is not enough.

### Sci-Fi / Fiction (17 books)

Curated, not sprawling. Mostly idea-driven fiction — the kind that changes how you think rather than how you feel. *Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy*, *Slaughterhouse-Five*, *Brave New World*, *Animal Farm*, *Atlas Shrugged*, *Snow Crash*, *Rendezvous with Rama*, *Solaris*, *Brothers Karamazov*, *Stories of Your Life and Others* by Ted Chiang. Every novel on the list is the kind that reshapes how you model the world — Chiang's stories reframe the nature of language and choice, Lem's *Solaris* reframes the nature of consciousness, Dostoevsky's *Brothers Karamazov* reframes the nature of faith.

### Psychology / Behavioral Science (19 books)

How people think, lie, decide, and sleep — direct fuel for product and leadership work. *Thinking in Bets* (two copies — the book that taught him to evaluate decisions by their quality, not their outcomes), *The Laws of Human Nature*, *Influence*, *Quiet*, *The Paradox of Choice*, *Why We Sleep* (the book that changed his health practices during the 2025 transformation), *Incognito*, and *The Man Who Mistook His Wife For a Hat*.

### History / Geopolitics / Biography (30+ books)

Heavy on India, Israel, North Korea, and individual genius. The India shelf includes *The Last Mughal*, *City of Djinns*, *Our Moon has Blood Clots* (Kashmir — a personal connection; see [[personal-stories]]), *Bhagat Singh: Why I Am An Atheist*, and *Field Marshal Sam Manekshaw*. The world history collection spans *Sapiens*, *The Rise and Fall of the Third Reich*, *Chip War*, *Start-Up Nation*, and the Israel intelligence shelf (*Mossad*, *Israel's Edge: Talpiot*). ### Self-Help / Productivity / Mindset (50+ books)

The largest category by count — the operating system. *Atomic Habits*, *The 4-Hour Workweek*, *Getting Things Done* (two copies), *Range*, *Principles* (two copies), *Extreme Ownership*, *Never Split the Difference*, *Clear Thinking*, *The Almanack of Naval Ravikant*, and *Ikigai* are among the highlights. This shelf is not self-help in the pejorative sense. It is the toolkit of a person who takes personal optimization seriously — who treats his own habits, decisions, and energy as systems to be understood and improved.

### Systems Thinking (13 books)

Not a subsection of something else — a distinct intellectual commitment that runs through everything Shravan builds and teaches. *Thinking in Systems* by Donella Meadows (the entry point — the book that teaches you to see feedback loops everywhere), *The Systems Bible* (two copies — providing the cynical counterweight: systems are not just elegant, they also fail in predictable, frustrating ways), *The Fifth Discipline* by Peter Senge, *Out of Control* by Kevin Kelly, *Limits to Growth*, and *Consilience*. These 13 books are not a reading list but a curriculum that maps directly to how he teaches [[system-dynamics-for-pms]] in the MPM cohort, where he reveals in Week 7 that the entire seven weeks of teaching was system dynamics in disguise. ### Additional Shelves

The collection also includes marketing and growth books (7, led by *Ogilvy on Advertising* and *Purple Cow*), writing and creativity (6, anchored by *On Writing* by Stephen King), and a surprisingly deep humor shelf (14 books, with Oscar Wilde appearing three times — a hero whose wit and social commentary clearly resonate).

### What the Library Reveals

The PM books are tools; the rest is who he is. Product management represents maybe 35 of 435 books — under 10%. The spiritual collection (35+), mathematics shelf (30+), and systems thinking library (13) dwarf the professional section. This is a person who became a PM because of how he thinks, not a person who thinks the way he does because of PM. The causality runs in a specific direction, and the library makes that direction unmistakable.

Mathematics is an obsession, not a curiosity. Fifteen books on prime numbers alone. Two copies of *Perfect Rigor*. The heroes are visible through the duplicates: Feynman (2 copies), Keanu Reeves (2 books), Oscar Wilde (3 books), Marcus Aurelius (2 copies), Kevin Kelly (2 books across categories), Ryan Holiday (6 books — the complete shelf). These are not casual reads; these are people he returns to, whose thinking has become part of his own.

Systems thinking is a worldview, not a skill. India runs through everything — Kashmir, Delhi, Chanakya, Bhagat Singh, Kabir, Mirabai. The fiction shelf is entirely idea-driven — Ted Chiang, Lem, Heinlein, Huxley, Dostoevsky, Clarke. Every novel changes how you model the world.

### The Gita Alongside Cagan: A Deliberate Shelf Architecture

The most distinctive feature of Shravan's book recommendations is not what he recommends but how he positions those recommendations relative to each other. In every curated book list he has ever posted — and he has posted the same core list eight or more times from 2022 to 2026 — the Bhagavad Gita As It Is sits alongside *Inspired* by Marty Cagan, *High Output Management* by Andy Grove, and *Radical Candor* by Kim Scott. Never in a separate "spiritual" section. Always in the main list.

The positioning is philosophically loaded. It is the claim that spiritual wisdom and professional excellence are not separate domains but complementary lenses on the same problems. The Gita's emphasis on dharma maps to his teaching on founder mentality. Its emphasis on karuna maps directly to the Compassion Funnel. Its emphasis on detachment from outcomes maps to "be passionate about the problem space, not the compensation." Shravan does not toggle between spiritual and professional — he treats them as a unified knowledge system. See [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]] for the full treatment.

### Specific Book Recommendations He Returns To

Certain books appear not just in lists but in Shravan's active teaching, repeatedly cited across cohorts, podcasts, and LinkedIn posts. *On Writing* by Stephen King is recommended not as a writing book but as a PM book: "PMs need to be great writers." His argument is that a product manager's primary output is communication, and the discipline of clear prose is what separates mediocre from excellent. *Team of Rivals* is cited as "one of the best books written on how to influence without authority" — the single most valuable skill for a PM who must align stakeholders who do not report to them. *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* is recommended for the emotional reality of building that business school case studies never capture.

For systems thinking, his recommendations form a progression: *Thinking in Systems* as the entry point, *The Systems Bible* as the cynical counterweight, *The Fifth Discipline* as the organizational extension, and *Out of Control* as the biological and technological frontier.

### The 50/50 Reading Rule

Shravan's reading advice to students is specific and counterintuitive: "Spend 50 percent of your time reading PM books but spend 50 percent of your time doing what you truly enjoy." The rule is derived from his own experience. The PM books gave him frameworks and vocabulary. But the Egyptian mythology, the Urdu mushairas, the fifteen books on prime numbers, the Oscar Wilde collection, the Ted Chiang stories — those gave him the generalist perspective that makes his teaching distinctive. His case studies draw on Age of Empires, the Korean stranger's kindness, Bollywood references, and mathematical proofs. None of that comes from the PM canon. All of it comes from the other 50%.

The 435-book library is the physical evidence of this rule in practice. PM and business books represent under 10% of the total. The rest — the spiritual collection, the mathematics, the systems thinking, the science fiction, the psychology, the history — is the infrastructure of a generalist mind that happens to do product management. The generalism is not a hobby. It is the competitive advantage.

## Impact

Books are Shravan's curriculum. His entire teaching philosophy at Rethink Systems — first principles, empathy, systems thinking — traces back to specific books. The Bhagavad Gita reshaped his life in 2020. The PM canon shaped his career. The wildcards — Stephen King on writing, Donella Meadows on systems, Ted Chiang on imagination — shaped his voice.

Reading, for him, is not preparation for work. It is the work. The 435-book Apple Books inventory confirms what the LinkedIn posts hinted at: the professional shelf is a small fraction. The real library — spiritual texts, mathematics, systems theory, behavioral science, history — is the infrastructure of a generalist mind that happens to do product management. The Gita alongside Cagan is not juxtaposition for effect. It is the honest representation of a mind where those two books solve the same problem from different angles, and the person who reads both sees things that the person who reads only one never will.
