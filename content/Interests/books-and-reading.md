> "I have an unreasonable number of books. Happy to trade recommendations." -- LinkedIn bio

## Context

Books are not a hobby for Shravan — they are infrastructure. He grew up as a voracious, undirected reader (Egyptian mythology, Hilbert's problems, Urdu mushairas) long before he found product management. His LinkedIn bio leads with books, not credentials. He curates and re-shares his reading lists obsessively, sometimes reposting the same book list across years with small updates, because he genuinely believes the right book at the right time changes careers.

His relationship with reading is deeply personal. As a kid in Jammu, while everyone expected him to study for boards, he was reading about the design of mathematics. In college at IIT Roorkee, he went to Urdu mushairas in Delhi instead of focusing on metallurgy. He calls himself a "generalist who loved doing whatever interested me" — and reading was the engine of that generalism.

## Details

### The Sacred Text

**The Bhagavad Gita As It Is** by A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada sits at the top of every list, always. Not in a "spirituality" section — right alongside product management books. He credits it with transforming his life: "In 2020, I read the BG, in which Lord Krsna spoke that compassion was the key to happiness, when you help others without expectation — bliss manifests." This was the book that broke his cycle of depression and imposter syndrome. It is not just a recommendation; it is the foundation of his entire life philosophy. See [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]] for how this text shapes his work.

### The PM Canon

Shravan has published detailed PM book lists repeatedly since 2022. The titles that appear most consistently form a clear canon. In **product thinking**, he returns again and again to *Creativity, Inc* by Ed Catmull ("putting 10x into practice"), *Made to Stick* by Chip and Dan Heath, *The Lean Product Playbook* by Dan Olsen ("one of the best books for learning product management for aspirants"), *In The Plex* by Steven Levy, and *Inspired* by Marty Cagan. For **product practice**, the essentials are *Continuous Discovery Habits* by Teresa Torres, *Getting To Yes* ("the best book on negotiation I have seen"), *Sprint* by Jake Knapp, and *Getting Things Done* by David Allen. In **product leadership**, *High Output Management* by Andy Grove ("still a classic"), *Empowered* by Marty Cagan, and *Product Leadership* by Banfield and Eriksson anchor the shelf.

His **design thinking** recommendations center on *Don't Make Me Think* by Steve Krug, *The Design of Everyday Things* by Don Norman ("still a classic, will always be a classic"), and *The Design Thinking Playbook* by Michael Lewrick. For **culture and people**, he recommends *Radical Candor* by Kim Scott, *No Rules Rules* by Reed Hastings, and *The Captain Class* by Sam Walker. And then there are the wildcards — non-PM books he insists PMs must read: *On Writing* by Stephen King ("PMs need to be great writers"), *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* by Ben Horowitz, *Team of Rivals* ("one of the best books written on how to influence without authority"), *Start With Why* by Simon Sinek, and *Chatter* by Ethan Kross.

### The Extended Library

In a May 2022 post, he published a much deeper list organized by discipline — Analytics, Business, Design, Engineering, Product Management — recommending 30+ books including *Blue Ocean Strategy*, *Algorithms to Live By*, *100 Things Every Designer Needs to Know About People*, *Behave* by Robert Sapolsky, *Chaos: Making a New Science* by James Gleick, *The Mythical Man-Month* ("old yet relevant"), *Escaping the Build Trap* by Melissa Perri, and *Business Model Generation* by Alexander Osterwalder. Honorable mentions that he references but does not always include in the essential lists are *Hooked* by Nir Eyal, *Crossing the Chasm*, and *Built to Last*.

### Reading as Identity

His childhood reading pattern reveals something important: he was never a disciplined reader. He read what fascinated him — mythology, mathematics, music, religion — and the breadth became his superpower. "I was always a generalist who loved doing whatever interested me." Reading across domains is what eventually made him a PM who can teach with examples from Age of Empires, the Bhagavad Gita, Korean kindness, and startup salary negotiations in the same breath.

### Articles He Recommends

Beyond books, Shravan curates essential article lists that he reshares regularly: "In Defense of Eye Candy" (design), "TikTok and the Sorting Hat" (product), "The Psychology of Design" from A List Apart, "How Superhuman Built an Engine to Find Product/Market Fit", and "What Buddhism Taught Me About Product Management" by Lenny Rachitsky.

### The Gita Alongside Cagan: A Deliberate Shelf Architecture

The most distinctive feature of Shravan's book recommendations is not what he recommends but how he positions those recommendations relative to each other. In every curated book list he has ever posted -- and he has posted the same core list eight or more times from 2022 to 2026 -- the Bhagavad Gita As It Is sits alongside *Inspired* by Marty Cagan, *High Output Management* by Andy Grove, and *Radical Candor* by Kim Scott. Never in a separate "spiritual" section. Always in the main list or under "Miscellaneous." The positioning is deliberate and philosophically loaded: this is not religion separate from work but a professional resource on par with the best PM literature.

When he built the "Ask SwagwalaPM" custom GPT, he described it as a bot that would "drop wisdom (maybe even a Bhagavad Gita quote) when you need it most." When students in Batch 6 of the MPM cohort said "you have duped us into learning human values instead of product management," they were describing the effect of a curriculum where *Continuous Discovery Habits* by Teresa Torres and the principle of nishkama karma (action without attachment to results) are taught in the same session, not in adjacent sessions. Shravan does not toggle between spiritual and professional -- he treats them as a unified knowledge system. The Gita's emphasis on dharma (duty) maps to his teaching on founder mentality. Its emphasis on karuna (compassion) maps directly to the Compassion Funnel he teaches for product thinking. Its emphasis on detachment from outcomes maps to his advice that "passion and persistence is the key" and that PMs should "be passionate about the problem space, not the compensation." See [[prabhupada-and-iskcon]] for the full treatment of how these teachings shape his work.

### Specific Book Recommendations He Returns To

Certain books appear not just in lists but in Shravan's active teaching, repeatedly cited across cohorts, podcasts, and LinkedIn posts. *On Writing* by Stephen King is recommended not as a writing book but as a PM book: "PMs need to be great writers." His argument is that a product manager's primary output is communication -- PRDs, strategy documents, stakeholder emails -- and the discipline of clear prose is what separates a mediocre PM from an excellent one. *Team of Rivals* by Doris Kearns Goodwin is cited as "one of the best books written on how to influence without authority," which is the single most valuable skill for a PM who must align engineering, design, and business stakeholders who do not report to them. *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* by Ben Horowitz is recommended for anyone thinking about startups, because it captures the emotional reality of building -- the loneliness, the near-bankruptcy, the decisions that have no good option -- in a way that business school case studies never do.

For systems thinking, his recommendations are deeply specific. *Thinking in Systems* by Donella Meadows is the entry point -- the book that teaches you to see feedback loops everywhere. *The Systems Bible* (two copies in his Apple Books library) provides the cynical counterweight: systems are not just elegant, they also fail in predictable, frustrating ways. *The Fifth Discipline* by Peter Senge extends systems thinking to organizations. *Out of Control* by Kevin Kelly extends it to biological and technological ecosystems. The 13 systems thinking books in his collection are not a reading list but a curriculum that maps directly to how he teaches [[system-dynamics-for-pms]] in the MPM cohort, where he reveals in Week 7 that the entire seven weeks of teaching was system dynamics in disguise.

For psychology and behavioral science, the recommendations are chosen for their direct applicability to product work. *Thinking in Bets* by Annie Duke (two copies) teaches decision-making under uncertainty, which is the PM's daily reality. *Influence* by Robert Cialdini provides the frameworks for understanding why users do what they do. *Why We Sleep* by Matthew Walker changed Shravan's health practices during the 2025 transformation and informs his teaching on operator wellness. *The Laws of Human Nature* by Robert Greene provides the leadership lens for understanding stakeholder behavior. These are not casual reads -- each one maps to a specific skill that shows up in how he teaches.

### The 50/50 Reading Rule

Shravan's reading advice to students is specific and counterintuitive: "Spend 50 percent of your time reading PM books but spend 50 percent of your time doing what you truly enjoy." The rule is derived from his own experience. The PM books gave him frameworks and vocabulary. But the Egyptian mythology he read as a kid in Jammu, the Urdu mushairas he attended instead of studying metallurgy, the fifteen books on prime numbers, the Oscar Wilde collection, the Ted Chiang stories -- those gave him the generalist perspective that makes his teaching distinctive. His case studies draw on Age of Empires (simulation games as PM training), the Korean stranger's kindness (compassion as product sense), Bollywood references (the Munna Bhai MBBS presentation format in the cohort), and mathematical proofs (the PM Compute Gap framework). None of that comes from the PM canon. All of it comes from the other 50%.

The 435-book library is the physical evidence of this rule in practice. PM and business books represent maybe 35 of 435 -- under 10%. The spiritual collection (35+), the mathematics shelf (30+), the systems thinking library (13), the science fiction (17), the psychology (19), and the history and biography (30+) collectively dwarf the professional section. This is not a PM who reads broadly as a hobby. It is a PM whose entire professional identity was constructed by reading broadly first and discovering product management as the applied discipline that connected everything.

## Impact

Books are Shravan's curriculum. His entire teaching philosophy at Rethink Systems -- first principles, empathy, systems thinking -- traces back to specific books. The Bhagavad Gita reshaped his life in 2020, breaking the cycle of imposter syndrome that had followed him since IIT Roorkee. The PM canon shaped his career. The wildcards -- Stephen King on writing, Donella Meadows on systems, Ted Chiang on imagination -- shaped his voice.

Reading, for him, is not preparation for work. It is the work. The 435-book Apple Books inventory confirms what the LinkedIn posts hinted at: the professional shelf is a small fraction. The real library -- spiritual texts, mathematics, systems theory, behavioral science, history -- is the infrastructure of a generalist mind that happens to do product management. The Gita alongside Cagan is not juxtaposition for effect. It is the honest representation of a mind where those two books solve the same problem from different angles, and the person who reads both sees things that the person who reads only one never will.
