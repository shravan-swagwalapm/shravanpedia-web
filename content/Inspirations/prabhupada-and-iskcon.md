> Srila Prabhupada's teachings and ISKCON — the spiritual foundation beneath everything Shravan builds.

## Context

This is not a hobby or side interest. **Prabhupada's teachings** are the bedrock upon which Shravan's entire professional and personal identity rests. He built an entire RAG application ([[prabhupada-ai]]) to make these teachings accessible through AI. He has created ISKCON leadership presentations. The spiritual practice informs his work ethic, his teaching philosophy, and how he thinks about service. Across 71 LinkedIn posts that reference spirituality and the Gita, a clear pattern emerges: Shravan does not compartmentalize the spiritual and the professional. They are one system. The Gita is not something he reads on weekends and forgets on Monday mornings. It is the operating system that runs beneath every product decision, every teaching session, every act of building.

## Details

### The Encounter That Changed Everything

In 2020, during one of the most turbulent periods in recent memory, Shravan picked up the Bhagavad Gita As It Is by A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada. He had read widely before — Egyptian mythology, Hilbert's problems, Urdu mushairas, Stoic philosophy — but nothing had reached the layer of consciousness that the Gita reached. "In 2020, I read the BG, in which Lord Krsna spoke that compassion was the key to happiness, when you help others without expectation — bliss manifests." What followed was not a sudden conversion but a gradual deepening — a growing awareness that the principles Krishna laid out five thousand years ago on the battlefield of Kurukshetra were speaking directly to the struggles of building products, teaching students, and navigating a career in technology.

The Gita became the book that restructured how Shravan understood purpose. It reframed his career ambitions away from compensation and titles and toward the question that Krishna poses to Arjuna: what is your dharma? What is the work that is yours to do, regardless of what rewards it brings? That question, once internalized, changed everything — from how he designed products to how he priced his courses to how he signed off every LinkedIn post.

### PrabhupadaAI

The most direct expression of this devotion is a full **RAG web app** grounded in Prabhupada's teachings. Built on FastAPI and Next.js, deployed on Railway with SQLite and FAISS for vector search, and featuring ElevenLabs voice synthesis that lets users hear answers in Prabhupada's own voice, the app turns scripture search into a living, accessible experience. Users ask spiritual questions in natural language, and the system searches across the Bhagavad Gita, Srimad Bhagavatam, and recorded lectures, returning AI-generated answers with source citations traced directly to actual verses and purports. Nothing is hallucinated wisdom — everything traces back to scripture.

The technical sophistication of the app — three-tier answer resolution, a Scripture Source Graph with interactive d3-force visualization, SSE streaming with disconnect detection — belies its deeply devotional purpose. Shravan did not build this to showcase his engineering skills. He built it because the idea of making Prabhupada's teachings accessible to anyone with a spiritual question, at any hour, in a voice that carries the warmth and authority of the spiritual master himself, was too beautiful a seva to leave unbuilt. Pricing has been finalized and an admin console is planned. This is the project with the deepest personal meaning in Shravan's entire portfolio. See [[prabhupada-ai]] for the full technical and narrative treatment.

### ISKCON Leadership

Shravan has created presentations for **ISKCON leadership** contexts, exploring the intersection of spiritual service and technology. The work reflects a recurring theme that Prabhupada himself embodied: that devotion and modern tools are not in tension but can amplify each other. Prabhupada sailed to America in 1965 at the age of 69 with nothing but a trunk of books and an unshakeable conviction that Krishna's message belonged to the whole world, not just to India. He used every modern tool available — printing presses, record albums, airports — to spread ancient wisdom. Shravan sees his own work with AI tools as a continuation of that principle: use whatever technology exists to make the teachings accessible to whoever needs them.

### The Gita as a PM Book

In every curated book list Shravan has ever posted — and he has posted the same list eight or more times from 2022 to 2026 — the **Bhagavad Gita As It Is** sits alongside *Inspired* by Marty Cagan, *High Output Management* by Andy Grove, and *Radical Candor* by Kim Scott. Never in a separate "spiritual" section. Always in "Miscellaneous" or the main list. The positioning is deliberate and philosophically loaded: this is not religion separate from work but a professional resource on par with the best PM literature.

When he built the "Ask SwagwalaPM" custom GPT, he described it as a bot that would "drop wisdom (maybe even a Bhagavad Gita quote) when you need it most." The Gita is woven into his teaching voice, not presented as a separate domain. In the MPM cohort, students encounter Krishna's instructions on duty, detachment, and compassion in the same sessions where they learn about growth loops, design sprints, and product analytics. The integration is seamless because for Shravan it was never separate to begin with.

### Bhagavad Gita Principles in His PM Teaching

His Batch 6 graduation — 60+ students, a 7.5-hour final lecture — produced the statement that clearly moved him most: "This is not a product management program but a program deeply rooted in human values: resilience, persistence, and the principles of the Bhagavad Gita." He embraced this fully in his reflection: "After 6 amazing batches, I've realized that this community was never only about learning PM — it was always about something much bigger. It was about unlearning mistakes from the past, becoming a better human every day, forgiving yourself, and building a brighter future."

The Gita principles that surface most clearly in his teaching form a complete philosophical framework for product work. **Nishkama karma** (action without attachment to results), rooted in Bhagavad Gita 2.47 — "You have a right to perform your prescribed duty, but you are not entitled to the fruits of action" — is the principle he expresses as "Be passionate about the problem space, not the compensation." This is not motivational rhetoric. It is an operational instruction. When a PM is attached to the outcome — the promotion, the stock price, the user numbers — their judgment warps. They ship features that look good in quarterly reviews rather than features that serve users. Nishkama karma is the antidote: do the work because it is the right work, and the results will follow on their own schedule. This principle is explored in depth in [[nishkama-karma-and-building]].

**Resilience through dharma** draws from 2.14's instruction to tolerate the nonpermanent appearance of happiness and distress like the passing of seasons. Shravan captures this in his recurring teaching "How you do anything is how you do everything." The seasons will change — a launch will go well, a hire will leave, a feature will flop — but the PM who is rooted in dharma (duty) responds to each with the same steadiness. The 30,000 daily steps, the OMAD discipline, the 5:30 AM chanting — these are not health habits. They are dharma practices that build the equanimity Krishna describes.

**Compassion as the foundation of action** finds its scriptural source in Chapter 12, verses 13-14, where Krishna describes the devotee dear to Him as one who is "a kind friend to all living entities" and "free from false ego." Shravan formalized this into his "Compassion Funnel" framework for product thinking — the progression from compassion to curiosity to passion to critical thinking that he teaches as the real source of product sense. The funnel is not a cleverly rebranded empathy map. It is a philosophical claim: genuine concern for the suffering of others is where every great product begins.

**Humility before knowledge** echoes 18.63's instruction to deliberate fully and then act as one wishes — the Lord offers knowledge but does not compel; the student must choose to receive. This is reflected in Shravan's recurring observation: "Every conversation reminded me how much I still have to learn — and unlearn." The full mapping of Gita teachings to PM practice is traced in [[gita-principles-in-product-management]].

### Devotion in Public Posts

Shravan publicly attributes his success to God in his LinkedIn posts — not as a throwaway line but as the emotional anchor of his major reflections. His 2024 year-end post concluded: "My deepest gratitude to Lord Shiva, whose blessings have made everything possible. I remain, and will always be, his humble servant." During his 2025 health transformation, he wrote: "My mind slowed down. I started waking up at 5:30 AM. Chanting. Sitting quietly. Becoming more God-conscious." At the Rethink Systems Batch 3 graduation: "All my love and gratitude go to my wonderful students — and to my 'Mahadev,' who has made this program and community truly special."

The devotion intensified through his 2025 health journey, when a doctor's warning became the catalyst for a complete physical and spiritual reorientation. Daily chanting and God-consciousness became central to his recovery and his renewed sense of purpose. "It wasn't motivation that saved me. It was humility. Faith. And the refusal to quit." The daily practice — 5:30 AM waking, japa (chanting on beads), quiet sitting — follows the Vaishnava tradition as taught by Srila Prabhupada. The Gita's instruction on regulation in Chapter 6, verse 17 — "He who is regulated in his habits of eating, sleeping, recreation and work can mitigate all material pains by practicing the yoga system" — maps precisely onto the health transformation: OMAD, protein-heavy meals, 30K steps daily, and the internal shift toward God-consciousness that followed physical discipline. The body became the temple, and caring for it became an act of devotion. See [[the-spiritual-foundation]] for the full treatment of this practice.

### Compassion Funnel — Spirituality Applied to Product

His stray dog story from 2021 is the clearest bridge between spiritual values and product practice. Seeing an injured animal on the street triggered compassion — a visceral, immediate response that had nothing to do with product management. That compassion led to curiosity about the problem space: how many strays are there, what happens to them, who is responsible? Curiosity led to passion for scaling a solution, which led to building a curated pet adoption marketplace with the animal husbandry department. He named this the **Compassion Funnel** and framed it as the real source of product sense: "You don't need a lot of frameworks to become a better PM, you just have to be a better human to become a better PM." This is Gita philosophy in PM clothing — Krishna's teaching that compassion for all living entities is the mark of a devotee, applied to the question of where product ideas come from.

The Compassion Funnel reappears in every cohort. In the MPM mindset session, Shravan spends more time on compassion than on any other topic. He asks students to debate the difference between love and compassion, pushes them to confront whether pure selflessness exists, and resolves the question at the systems level: compassion means you believe the world is one system, and anything you do in the interest of the system will eventually benefit you. It is Krishna's instruction in Chapter 5, verse 29 — that the Lord is the beneficiary of all sacrifices and austerities, the Supreme Lord of all planets and demigods, and the benefactor and well-wisher of all living entities — applied to the question of why a PM should care about users they will never meet.

### Service Orientation

The "Shravan Bhaiya" persona is **seva** (service) channeled through content creation and teaching. He started TheSwagWalaPM to make PM education free and accessible. More than 200 PMs transitioned at zero cost. The framing is always "yours" — every post ends with "Yours, Shravan Bhaiya." The relationship is not teacher-student but elder-brother-family. "Whatever I am today, it's because of you" appears repeatedly, echoing the Vaishnava principle of seeing oneself as servant, not master.

This is not false humility. It is a deliberate inversion of the guru-disciple hierarchy that dominates Indian education and content creation. In the Vaishnava tradition, the guru is not elevated above the disciple but is the most dedicated servant of all. Prabhupada himself, despite founding a global movement, always referred to himself as the servant of his spiritual master, Bhaktisiddhanta Sarasvati Thakur. Shravan follows this pattern — positioning himself not as the expert dispensing knowledge from above, but as the older brother who learned things the hard way and wants to make the path easier for those who follow.

The spiritual foundation shapes the building in concrete, measurable ways. A service orientation prioritizes tools that serve others over pure monetization — PrabhupadaAI is priced for sustainability, not profit maximization. Patience and persistence echo spiritual discipline — the 700+ pages of the [[builders-bible]] were given away free because the Gita teaches that knowledge shared without expectation produces the deepest rewards. Teaching as seva runs through @TheSwagWalaPM's knowledge sharing, where the metric that matters is not subscriber count but lives changed. And [[prabhupada-ai]] itself is the most direct expression — technology as a vessel for making ancient wisdom accessible to a world that needs it now more than ever.

### The Mind as Instrument

Bhagavad Gita 6.5-6 teaches that the mind is both the friend and the enemy of the conditioned soul: "For him who has conquered the mind, the mind is the best of friends; but for one who has failed to do so, his mind will remain the greatest enemy." Prabhupada's purport explains that unless the mind is controlled, the practice of yoga is simply a waste of time, and one who cannot control his mind lives always with the greatest enemy. For Shravan, who manages multiple active projects, a teaching company, a content brand, and angel investments simultaneously, this is not philosophy but daily operational reality.

The morning chanting practice is the mechanism of mind-conquest — the decision, renewed daily, to make the mind a servant of prescribed duty before it is asked to serve as a tool for worldly work. Before the first Claude Code session opens, before the first LinkedIn post is written, the mind has already been directed toward service and surrender. The equanimity visible in his public persona — steady through success, steady through difficulty — traces to the sthita-dhir muni (sage of steady mind) described in 2.56: "One who is not disturbed in mind even amidst the threefold miseries or elated when there is happiness, and who is free from attachment, fear and anger, is called a sage of steady mind." This is not a distant ideal. It is the daily practice of a builder who has learned that the mind, once conquered, becomes the most powerful tool in the entire stack.

### Surrender as the Final Instruction

The Gita's conclusion in 18.66 — "Abandon all varieties of religion and just surrender unto Me. I shall deliver you from all sinful reactions. Do not fear." — is the verse that ties the entire spiritual foundation together. Prabhupada's purport explains that simple surrender makes all other purificatory processes unnecessary, and that one should unhesitatingly accept Krishna as the supreme savior with faith and love. Shravan's public attributions to God — "I remain, and will always be, his humble servant" — are not rhetorical gestures but the external expressions of this act of surrender.

The sign-off "Yours, Shravan Bhaiya" encodes the Vaishnava principle of seeing oneself as servant. The building, the teaching, the content creation — all of it flows downstream from this daily renewal of saranagati (surrender). When a new cohort begins, when a product launches, when a student gets a job — the response is always gratitude directed upward. The success is grace. The work is duty. The surrender is the frame that holds both.

There is a profound freedom in this orientation. When you are not attached to the fruits of your action, you can build with more courage, teach with more honesty, and give with more generosity. You do not need every cohort to be bigger than the last. You do not need every project to go viral. You need only to do the work that is yours to do, with the fullness of your capability, and leave the results to a wisdom greater than your own.

## Impact

Prabhupada's teachings are the "why" behind much of Shravan's work. PrabhupadaAI is the most direct expression, but the influence runs through everything — the Gita sitting alongside Marty Cagan in every book list, the Compassion Funnel framework, the devotional sign-offs, the students who said "you duped us into learning human values." The spiritual and the professional are not two tracks for Shravan. They are one practice, expressed through building products, teaching PMs, and showing up with humility every day. In a world of tech creators who optimize for engagement metrics, Shravan optimizes for something the Gita calls sukham atyantikam — the supreme, transcendental happiness that comes from serving others with a surrendered heart.
